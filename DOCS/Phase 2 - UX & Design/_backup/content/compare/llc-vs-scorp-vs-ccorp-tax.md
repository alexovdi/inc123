# LLC vs S-Corp vs C-Corp: Tax Treatment Compared

The entity type you choose determines how your business income is taxed, how much you pay in self-employment and payroll taxes, and what deductions and strategies are available to you. This is not a formation decision — it is a tax election that you make with the IRS after your entity is formed. Understanding the differences is essential because the wrong election can cost thousands of dollars per year in unnecessary taxes.

**Important:** This comparison provides general educational information. Tax situations vary significantly based on income level, state of residence, business type, and personal circumstances. Consult a qualified tax professional before making your election.

## LLC (Default Tax Treatment)

When you form an LLC and make no tax election, the IRS treats it as a "disregarded entity" (single-member) or a "partnership" (multi-member). In either case, all net business income passes through to your personal tax return and is taxed at your individual rate.

**The self-employment tax issue.** The biggest tax consideration for default LLCs is self-employment tax. All net business income is subject to the 15.3% self-employment tax (12.4% Social Security up to the wage base, plus 2.9% Medicare on all income). For a business earning $100,000 in net profit, that is approximately $15,300 in self-employment tax alone — on top of your regular income tax.

**When default LLC taxation works best:**
- Businesses in their first year or two that are still establishing revenue
- Businesses with net profit under approximately $40,000-$50,000
- Passive income businesses (rental real estate, investments) where self-employment tax may not apply
- Business owners who value simplicity and want minimal tax filing complexity

**The 20% QBI deduction.** Qualifying LLC income may be eligible for the Section 199A Qualified Business Income deduction, which allows you to deduct up to 20% of qualified business income from your taxable income. This deduction phases out at higher income levels for certain service businesses but provides meaningful tax savings for qualifying businesses.

## S-Corporation Tax Election

An S-Corporation is not a separate entity type — it is a tax election that an LLC or Corporation can make with the IRS by filing Form 2553. The entity remains an LLC (or Corporation) under state law but is taxed under Subchapter S of the Internal Revenue Code.

**How the tax savings work.** With an S-Corp election, you split your business income into two categories: salary (which is subject to payroll taxes) and distributions (which are not subject to self-employment tax). The IRS requires that you pay yourself a "reasonable salary" for the work you perform, but any profit above that salary can be taken as a distribution free of the 15.3% self-employment tax.

**Example:** A business earns $150,000 in net profit. The owner pays themselves a reasonable salary of $70,000.

- **As a default LLC:** $150,000 is subject to self-employment tax = approximately $21,200 in SE tax
- **As an S-Corp:** $70,000 salary is subject to payroll tax ($10,710), and $80,000 in distributions has no SE tax. Total payroll tax = approximately $10,710
- **Annual savings:** approximately $10,490

**When S-Corp election works best:**
- Active businesses with consistent net profit above $60,000-$80,000
- Business owners who can justify splitting income between salary and distributions
- Businesses where the administrative cost of payroll ($500-$2,000/year) is offset by tax savings
- Businesses with one or a few owners who are all U.S. citizens or residents

**Trade-offs.** S-Corp election requires running payroll (including quarterly payroll tax filings), maintaining proper corporate minutes and records, and paying yourself a reasonable salary. The IRS actively audits S-Corp salary levels — paying yourself too little to maximize distributions can trigger penalties. There are also ownership restrictions: no more than 100 shareholders, no non-U.S. shareholders, and no corporate or partnership shareholders.

## C-Corporation Tax Election

A C-Corporation is taxed as a separate entity from its owners. The corporation pays federal income tax at a flat 21% rate on its profits, and when those profits are distributed to shareholders as dividends, the shareholders pay tax again at their individual rate (typically 15-20% for qualified dividends). This is commonly called "double taxation."

**When C-Corp makes sense despite double taxation:**
- Businesses that plan to reinvest most profits rather than distribute them. The 21% corporate rate is lower than the top individual rate of 37%, so retaining earnings in the corporation and reinvesting creates a deferral advantage.
- Businesses seeking outside investment. Venture capital, private equity, and many institutional investors require C-Corp structure.
- Businesses planning for an eventual sale or IPO, where C-Corp stock sale treatment may be more favorable.
- Businesses with international operations where the C-Corp structure provides more flexibility.

**When C-Corp does not make sense:**
- Small businesses that distribute most or all profits to owners. Double taxation makes this the most expensive option.
- Solo entrepreneurs and small partnerships where pass-through taxation (LLC or S-Corp) is almost always more tax-efficient.
- Businesses where the QBI deduction (available to LLCs and S-Corps but not C-Corps) provides meaningful savings.

## Our Recommendation

**For most small business owners, start with a default LLC and switch to S-Corp election when net profits consistently exceed $60,000-$80,000.** This approach provides simplicity in the early years and tax savings once your income justifies the additional administrative costs of running payroll.

The tax election is separate from the formation decision. Form your LLC in the state that provides the best privacy and asset protection (we recommend Wyoming), and then work with your tax professional to determine the optimal tax election based on your specific income, deductions, and business structure.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I change my tax election later?**
Yes. You can elect S-Corp status on an existing LLC by filing Form 2553. You can also revoke an S-Corp election. C-Corp to S-Corp conversions have a waiting period. Tax election changes should be planned with a tax professional.

**Does my formation state affect my tax election?**
No. Federal tax treatment is determined by your IRS election, not by the state of formation. A Wyoming LLC and a Nevada LLC have identical federal tax options.

**What is a "reasonable salary" for an S-Corp?**
The IRS requires S-Corp owner-employees to receive compensation comparable to what they would earn in a similar position at a similar company. There is no fixed formula, but factors include industry norms, hours worked, responsibilities, and the company's revenue. Setting salary too low is the most common S-Corp audit trigger.

**Do I need to form a Corporation to be taxed as an S-Corp?**
No. An LLC can elect S-Corp taxation by filing Form 2553. The entity remains an LLC under state law with all of its flexibility and protections, but it is taxed as an S-Corp for federal purposes.

**What about the QBI deduction?**
The Section 199A Qualified Business Income deduction allows eligible LLCs and S-Corps to deduct up to 20% of qualified business income. This deduction is not available to C-Corps. For qualifying businesses, this deduction significantly reduces the effective tax rate on pass-through income.

**When should I talk to a tax professional?**
Before making your tax election, and annually as your business grows. The optimal structure often changes as income increases. A $2,000 tax planning session that saves $10,000 per year in taxes is the best investment you can make.
