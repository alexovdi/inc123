# Entity Domestication

If you already have an LLC or corporation formed in one state and want to move it to Wyoming or Nevada, you do not need to dissolve the old entity and start over. Domestication allows you to transfer your entity from one state to another while preserving its legal identity, history, EIN, contracts, bank accounts, and all existing relationships.

Domestication is different from foreign registration. Foreign registration lets your entity operate in a new state while remaining formed in the original state — you end up registered in two states. Domestication actually moves the entity: the original state registration is terminated, and the entity continues as a domestic entity in the new state. Same entity, same history, new home state.

There are several reasons a business owner might want to move an existing entity to Wyoming or Nevada:

### Better Privacy Protections

If your current state requires public disclosure of LLC members, managers, or ownership interests, domesticating to Wyoming or Nevada can significantly improve your privacy. Wyoming does not require disclosure of LLC members on formation documents. Nevada allows nominee officers and directors, keeping your personal name entirely off public records.

### Stronger Asset Protection

Wyoming and Nevada offer some of the strongest asset protection statutes in the country. Wyoming's charging order protection — the exclusive remedy for creditors seeking to reach a member's LLC interest — is among the most protective in the nation. If your current state has weaker protections, domestication gives you access to a better legal framework.

### Lower Ongoing Costs

Some states impose high annual fees, franchise taxes, or reporting requirements. California charges every LLC $800/year in franchise tax regardless of revenue. If you can legitimately domesticate to Wyoming (where the annual report is $62) or Nevada, you may reduce your ongoing compliance costs significantly.

### Simplified Structure

If you formed in one state but have been operating primarily through a foreign registration in another, domestication can simplify your structure. Instead of maintaining registrations in two states, you domesticate to the state that offers the best combination of laws, privacy, and cost for your situation.

### Continuity

Unlike dissolving and reforming, domestication preserves everything about your entity. Your EIN stays the same. Your contracts remain in effect. Your bank accounts continue uninterrupted. Your entity's formation date is recognized as the original date — the entity is treated as having existed continuously from its original creation.

## How Domestication Works

Domestication is governed by statute in each participating state. In Nevada, the process is defined under NRS 92A.270 — _in plain English, this statute establishes the legal mechanism for an entity formed in another state to become a Nevada domestic entity while maintaining its original legal identity, formation date, and all existing contracts and obligations_. Wyoming has its own domestication provisions under its Business Entities Act. Both states allow "inbound" domestication — meaning they accept entities moving in from other states.

The general process involves:

### 1. Verify Eligibility

Not every state allows outbound domestication — some states do not permit an entity to transfer out. We verify that your current state allows the entity to leave and that the destination state (Wyoming or Nevada) accepts the entity type you are transferring.

### 2. Obtain Approval

The domestication must be approved according to your entity's governing documents (operating agreement or bylaws) and the laws of your current state. This typically requires a vote or written consent from the members or shareholders.

### 3. Obtain Certificate of Good Standing

You must provide a certified copy of your entity's charter document from the current state and a certificate of good standing confirming the entity is active and in compliance.

### 4. File Articles of Domestication

We prepare and file the articles of domestication with the Secretary of State in the destination state. This filing includes:

- The date and jurisdiction where the entity was originally formed
- The entity's current name and the name it will use in the new state
- The type of entity (LLC, corporation, etc.)
- The jurisdiction of the entity's principal place of business
- The appropriate charter document for the new state (Articles of Organization or Incorporation)
- The registered agent designation in the new state

### 5. File Withdrawal in Original State

Once the domestication is approved in the new state, we file a withdrawal or termination notice in the original state, formally ending the entity's registration there.

### 6. Set Up Under New State Laws

After domestication, your entity is governed by the new state's laws. We prepare updated governing documents — a new operating agreement or bylaws that conform to Wyoming or Nevada statutes — and set up your registered agent, offshore secretary, and compliance tracking in the new state.

## What Is Preserved During Domestication

This is the key advantage of domestication over dissolving and reforming:

- **Entity identity** — The domesticated entity is legally the same entity. It is not a new company.
- **Formation date** — The entity's existence is dated from its original formation, not from the domestication date.
- **EIN** — Your federal Employer Identification Number remains the same.
- **Contracts** — All existing contracts, agreements, and obligations carry forward automatically.
- **Bank accounts** — Your entity's bank accounts continue without interruption (you may need to update state of formation records with the bank).
- **Property** — Title to real property and other assets is not affected. No transfer deeds are needed.
- **Debts and obligations** — All existing debts and liabilities attach to the domesticated entity in the new state.
- **Legal proceedings** — Pending lawsuits and legal claims continue against the same entity.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between domestication and foreign registration?

Domestication moves your entity from one state to another — you end up with a single registration in the new state. Foreign registration lets your entity operate in an additional state while remaining formed in the original — you end up registered in two states with compliance obligations in both.

### Does domestication change my EIN?

No. Your EIN remains the same because the entity is legally the same entity. You are changing the state of formation, not creating a new company.

### Can any entity type be domesticated?

Most states allow domestication of LLCs and corporations. Some states also allow limited partnerships and other entity types. Not all states participate in domestication — some do not allow entities to transfer out. We verify eligibility before beginning the process.

### How long does domestication take?

Typically 2-6 weeks, depending on the processing times in both the original and destination states. Expedited processing is available in most states for an additional fee.

### How much does domestication cost?

Our service fee for managing the domestication process varies based on the complexity of your specific situation. State filing fees typically range from $75 to $500, depending on the states involved. We provide a detailed quote after reviewing your entity's specifics.

### Will domestication affect my existing contracts?

No. All contracts, agreements, and obligations carry forward automatically. The domesticated entity is legally the same entity, so all existing agreements remain in effect without any need for amendment or re-execution.

### Can I domesticate from California to Wyoming to avoid the franchise tax?

Only if your entity will genuinely no longer be "doing business" in California after domestication. California broadly defines "doing business" to include having members who reside in the state. If you domesticate to Wyoming but continue operating in California, you may still owe the franchise tax. We discuss these implications during consultation.

### Do I need to notify my bank?

Yes. After domestication, you should update your entity's state of formation with your bank. Most banks handle this as an administrative update. Your account numbers, balances, and access remain unchanged.

### What happens to pending lawsuits?

All existing legal claims and proceedings continue against the domesticated entity. Domestication does not affect or discharge any obligations or liabilities that existed before the transfer.

### Does the original state need to approve the domestication?

The domestication must comply with the laws of the original state and be approved according to your entity's governing documents. Some states require a filing to formally acknowledge the entity's departure. We handle all required filings in both states.

**Can I domesticate an entity that is not in good standing?**
You must bring your entity into good standing in the original state before domestication can proceed. We can help file any missing reports and pay outstanding fees to restore good standing as a first step.

**What happens to my business licenses in the original state?**
State and local business licenses in the original state are typically terminated as part of the withdrawal process. If you continue operating in that state after domestication, you will need a foreign registration and any applicable local licenses in that state.

**Is domestication the same as a merger?**
No. A merger combines two entities into one. Domestication moves a single entity from one state to another without combining it with anything. The entity remains the same legal person with the same history — it simply changes its state of formation.
