# Entity Tax Guide: LLC vs. S-Corp vs. C-Corp

The entity type you choose for your business determines how your income is taxed at the federal level. This decision can mean thousands of dollars in annual tax savings -- or thousands wasted. Understanding the differences between sole proprietorship taxation, LLC pass-through taxation, S-Corp election, and C-Corp taxation is essential before you form your business.

This guide covers each structure, explains when one is better than another, and provides the specific income thresholds where switching structures makes financial sense. This is educational information, not tax advice. Work with a qualified tax professional to apply these principles to your specific situation.

### Sole Proprietorship (Default for Individuals)

If you operate a business without forming an entity, you are a sole proprietor by default. All business income flows directly to your personal tax return on Schedule C. You pay income tax at your marginal personal rate plus self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare) of 15.3% on the first $168,600 of net earnings (2024 threshold) and 2.9% on amounts above that.

For a sole proprietor earning $100,000 in net business income, self-employment tax alone is approximately $14,130 -- before income tax. There is no separation between personal and business assets, which means unlimited personal liability for business debts and lawsuits.

### Single-Member LLC (Default Taxation)

A single-member LLC is taxed exactly like a sole proprietorship by default. The IRS treats it as a "disregarded entity," meaning all income flows to Schedule C on your personal return. You pay the same income tax and self-employment tax as a sole proprietor.

The difference is legal, not tax-related. An LLC creates a liability shield between your personal assets and your business obligations. You get the same tax treatment as a sole proprietor but with the asset protection of a formal entity.

### Multi-Member LLC (Default Taxation)

A multi-member LLC is taxed as a partnership by default. The LLC files an informational return (Form 1065), and each member receives a Schedule K-1 showing their share of income, deductions, and credits. Each member then reports this on their personal return and pays income tax and self-employment tax on their share.

Partnership taxation allows flexible allocation of income and losses among members, as specified in the operating agreement. This flexibility is one of the primary reasons business partners choose LLCs over corporations.

### S-Corporation (Tax Election)

An S-Corp is not a separate entity type -- it is a tax election. You can form an LLC or a corporation and then elect S-Corp taxation by filing IRS Form 2553. The entity is still an LLC or corporation under state law, but the IRS treats it as an S-Corp for tax purposes.

Here is how S-Corp taxation works:

1. The business pays you a "reasonable salary" for the work you perform. This salary is subject to payroll taxes (Social Security and Medicare), which total 15.3% split between employer and employee portions.
2. Any profit above your reasonable salary is distributed to you as a shareholder distribution. This distribution is subject to income tax but NOT subject to self-employment/payroll tax.

This is where the savings come from. If your LLC earns $150,000 in net income and you pay yourself a reasonable salary of $70,000, the remaining $80,000 in distributions avoids the 15.3% self-employment tax. That is approximately $12,240 in annual savings.

**S-Corp requirements:**

- 100 or fewer shareholders
- All shareholders must be U.S. citizens or permanent residents
- Only one class of stock allowed
- Must pay shareholders who work in the business a "reasonable salary"
- Requires payroll processing and additional tax filings (Form 1120-S)

### C-Corporation (Default for Corporations)

A C-Corp is a separate tax-paying entity. The corporation files its own tax return (Form 1120) and pays a flat 21% federal tax rate on corporate income. When the corporation distributes profits to shareholders as dividends, those dividends are taxed again at the shareholder's personal rate (qualified dividends are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on the shareholder's income bracket).

This is known as "double taxation" -- income is taxed once at the corporate level and again when distributed to shareholders.

C-Corp taxation makes sense in specific situations:

- **You plan to reinvest profits.** If the corporation retains earnings for growth rather than distributing them, only the 21% corporate rate applies. For business owners in the 32% or higher personal tax bracket, this means paying less tax on retained earnings.
- **You have significant fringe benefits.** C-Corps can deduct the cost of employee benefits (health insurance, retirement plans, education assistance) more broadly than pass-through entities.
- **You are building toward a sale or IPO.** Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) rules under Section 1202 allow C-Corp shareholders to exclude up to $10 million in capital gains when selling stock held for more than five years, provided the corporation meets specific criteria.

## When to Elect S-Corp Status

The S-Corp election makes financial sense when your net business income is high enough that the payroll tax savings on distributions exceed the additional costs of running an S-Corp (payroll processing, additional tax filings, and reasonable salary compliance).

**General threshold: $80,000+ in net business income.** Below this level, the administrative costs and complexity of S-Corp taxation typically offset the payroll tax savings. Above this level, the savings increase with each additional dollar of profit.

## State Tax Considerations

The entity type affects your federal tax treatment. State taxes add another layer:

**No state income tax:** Wyoming, Nevada, Texas, Florida, Washington, South Dakota, Alaska, and New Hampshire (limited). If you reside in one of these states and form your entity in Wyoming or Nevada, your business income faces only federal taxation.

**State income tax states:** If you live in a state with income tax (e.g., California, New York, New Jersey), you will owe state tax on your business income regardless of where your entity is formed. Forming a Wyoming LLC does not exempt you from your home state's income tax if you live and work there.

**Foreign registration:** If you form in Wyoming but operate in your home state, you may need to file a foreign registration in that state. This does not change your tax obligations -- it simply registers your entity with the state where you conduct business.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the difference between an LLC and an S-Corp?**
An LLC is an entity type formed under state law. An S-Corp is a tax election filed with the IRS. You can form an LLC and then elect S-Corp taxation -- the entity remains an LLC under state law but is taxed as an S-Corp at the federal level.

**When should I elect S-Corp status?**
Generally when your net business income exceeds $80,000 per year. Below that, the administrative costs offset the tax savings. Above that, the payroll tax savings on distributions grow with each additional dollar of profit.

**What is a "reasonable salary" for S-Corp owners?**
The salary must reflect what you would earn as an employee performing the same work in the same industry and location. There is no fixed percentage or formula. The IRS can reclassify distributions as wages if it determines the salary is unreasonably low.

**Does forming in Wyoming or Nevada eliminate my state income tax?**
Only if you reside in a state with no income tax. If you live in California, for example, you owe California income tax on your business income regardless of where the entity is formed.

**Can I switch from LLC to S-Corp taxation later?**
Yes. You can elect S-Corp taxation at any time by filing Form 2553 with the IRS. The election is effective for the tax year in which it is filed (if filed by March 15) or the following tax year.

**What is double taxation with a C-Corp?**
The corporation pays 21% federal tax on its income. When it distributes dividends to shareholders, those dividends are taxed again on the shareholder's personal return. The total tax burden on distributed income is higher than with pass-through entities.

**Is a multi-member LLC always taxed as a partnership?**
By default, yes. However, a multi-member LLC can elect S-Corp or C-Corp taxation by filing the appropriate IRS form.

**Do I need payroll if I elect S-Corp?**
Yes. You must run payroll for any owners who work in the business, withhold employment taxes, and file quarterly payroll returns. This adds administrative cost but is required for the S-Corp election to be valid.

**What is QSBS and does it apply to me?**
Qualified Small Business Stock (Section 1202) allows C-Corp shareholders to exclude up to $10 million in capital gains on stock held for more than five years, if the corporation's assets were under $50 million at the time of stock issuance. This primarily benefits founders and early investors in growth companies.

**Can a foreign national use S-Corp taxation?**
No. All S-Corp shareholders must be U.S. citizens or permanent residents. Non-resident aliens cannot hold S-Corp stock. An LLC taxed as a partnership or a C-Corp are the alternatives for foreign national owners.

**What tax forms does each entity type file?**
Sole proprietor/single-member LLC: Schedule C on Form 1040. Multi-member LLC: Form 1065 (partnership return). S-Corp: Form 1120-S. C-Corp: Form 1120.
