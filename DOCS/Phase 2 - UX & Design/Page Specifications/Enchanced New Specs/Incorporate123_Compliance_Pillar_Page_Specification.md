## 1. Page Purpose & Strategic Role

### 1.1 Primary Goal

Capture a customer segment the other three pillars miss: **existing entity owners** who already have an LLC or corporation and need ongoing maintenance, want to move an entity to a better state, or need to register in a new operating state. Secondary goal: reinforce to every Incorporate123 client that compliance is handled — reducing churn and building lifetime value.

### 1.2 Role Within Site Architecture

Compliance is the **fourth pillar** — the only pillar optimized for retention and post-sale relationship rather than net-new acquisition. It plays four distinct roles:

1. **New customer acquisition** — Existing entity owners searching for registered agent, annual report, domestication, or foreign registration services. These visitors have entities formed elsewhere and are looking for a better service provider. They represent a new revenue stream with no formation sale required.
2. **Cross-pillar capture** — Every other pillar page includes a compliance CTA: "Already have an entity? We handle compliance." This page is the destination for that CTA. It catches visitors from Privacy, AP, and Formation who don't need to form a new entity.
3. **Retention and LTV** — Every Incorporate123 package includes first-year registered agent service. The Compliance pillar educates clients on why they need to renew and what happens if they don't. This reduces churn and positions Incorporate123 as a long-term partner, not a one-time filing service.
4. **"Minimum necessary compliance"** — David's second main selling point. The page must communicate that Incorporate123 handles exactly what's required — no unnecessary filings, no upselling compliance services clients don't need — while maintaining the structural protections they've built.

### 1.3 Target Personas

| Persona | Arrival Mindset | What They Need From This Page |
|---------|----------------|-------------------------------|
| **Returning Client** (primary) | "I formed through you last year. What do I need to do now?" | Clear renewal information, login link, what's covered |
| **Existing Entity Owner** (secondary) | "I have an LLC in [another state]. I need a registered agent / want to move it to Wyoming." | Service discovery → domestication or RA service → conversion |
| **New Formation Client** (tertiary) | "I just formed an entity. What happens next?" | Post-formation orientation → what compliance is handled |
| **Cross-Pillar Visitor** (quaternary) | "I clicked 'Already have an entity?' from another pillar page." | Routing to the right compliance service |

### 1.4 Success Metrics

- **Primary:** Registered agent service sign-ups (new clients with existing entities)
- **Secondary:** Domestication and foreign registration inquiries/conversions
- **Tertiary:** Annual renewal retention rate (existing clients who renew)
- **Quaternary:** Click-through to cluster pages for detailed service information
- **Bounce target:** < 55% (compliance is a lower-intent pillar — visitors may be exploring, not buying)
- **Time on page target:** 2–3 minutes (compliance is functional, not educational — visitors want answers, not depth)

### 1.5 Strategic Distinction

Compliance is fundamentally different from the other three pillars:

| Dimension | Privacy / AP / Formation | Compliance |
|-----------|--------------------------|------------|
| Primary audience | Net-new prospects | Existing entity owners + returning clients |
| Conversion type | Formation package purchase | Service subscription (RA) or one-time service (domestication) |
| Content tone | Educational, persuasive | Practical, reassuring, service-oriented |
| Competitor landscape | Budget filers + attorney firms | Generic RA providers (Northwest, Incfile, LegalZoom) |
| Revenue model | One-time formation + annual renewal | Ongoing annual fees (retention-based) |
| Page pacing | Narrative arc with persuasion | Service catalog with clear routing |

## 2. Narrative Arc & Section Order

Compliance visitors arrive with a specific need — not a concept to explore. They already have an entity. They need a service. The page's arc is the shortest and most functional of all four pillars: validate → orient → route → convert. Less education, more wayfinding.

| # | Section | Narrative Role | Visitor Mindset at Entry |
|---|---------|---------------|--------------------------|
| 1 | Pillar Hero | **Hook** — Validate and reassure | "I need help keeping my entity compliant." |
| 2 | The Philosophy | **Frame** — Minimum necessary compliance | "What's your approach to compliance?" |
| 3 | What We Handle | **Orient** — Service overview grid | "What specific services do you offer?" |
| 4 | Cluster Navigation Grid | **Route** — Deep links to each service | "I need [specific service]." |
| 5 | Already Have an Entity? | **Capture** — Domestication + foreign reg | "I have an entity elsewhere. Can you help?" |
| 6 | What Happens If You Don't | **Motivate** — Consequences of non-compliance | "Is compliance really that important?" |
| 7 | Included in Every Package | **Reassure** — Existing clients | "What's already covered in my renewal?" |
| 8 | Social Proof Strip | **Validate** — Trust signals | "Can I trust you with ongoing compliance?" |
| 9 | FAQ Accordion | **Resolve** — Handle questions | "What about deadlines, costs, switching..." |
| 10 | Cross-Pillar Bridge | **Expand** — Surface formation + privacy | "I might also need to form an entity / add privacy." |
| 11 | Final CTA | **Close** — Drive action | "Let's get this handled." |

### Why This Order Works

**Section 1 (Hook):** Compliance visitors arrive with low anxiety but high specificity. The hero validates they're in the right place and immediately communicates the brand philosophy: we handle what's necessary, nothing more.

**Section 2 (Frame):** "Minimum necessary compliance" is David's second main selling point. This section defines the philosophy before listing services — it answers *how* Incorporate123 approaches compliance before *what* they offer. This is a differentiation moment: competitors either over-sell compliance services or under-deliver on them.

**Section 3 (Orient):** A compact service overview grid — not educational depth, just "here's what we do" with enough description to self-select. This is the section that tells the returning client "yes, your annual report is handled" and tells the new visitor "yes, we offer registered agent services."

**Section 4 (Route):** The cluster navigation grid routes to 9 detailed service pages. Compliance has the most cluster pages of any pillar in the v4 architecture (10 pages), and no comparison pages — compliance isn't a "compare options" domain.

**Section 5 (Capture):** Unique to Compliance — this section targets existing entity owners who want to move their entity to Wyoming or Nevada (domestication) or register in a new state (foreign registration). These are high-value conversions that require no formation sale.

**Section 6 (Motivate):** A brief "consequences" section for visitors who wonder if compliance really matters. Framed around veil piercing: if your entity falls out of compliance, the structural protections you've built (privacy, asset protection) can be voided. This connects compliance to the other three pillars.

**Section 7 (Reassure):** Existing Incorporate123 clients see what's already included in their renewal — no surprise that they need to buy more. This is a retention section, not an acquisition section.

**Sections 8–11 (Validate + Resolve + Expand + Close):** Standard pattern from the other pillar pages, adapted for compliance's functional tone.

A: Foreign registration allows your Wyoming or Nevada entity to

operations. Domestication physically moves the entity from one
state to another — it changes the state of incorporation entirely.
Foreign registration is for operating in additional states;
domestication is for changing your home state.

A: Domestication moves your entity from one state to another

state of incorporation changes. Consider domestication if your
current state has high annual fees (California's $800 minimum
