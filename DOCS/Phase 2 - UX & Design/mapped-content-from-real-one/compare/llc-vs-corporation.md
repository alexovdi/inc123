# LLC vs Corporation

Choosing between an LLC and a Corporation is one of the first decisions you will make when forming a business entity, and it affects everything from your personal liability exposure to your tax obligations to how you manage the business day-to-day. Both entity types provide liability protection, but they do it through fundamentally different legal structures with different levels of flexibility, different governance requirements, and different asset protection mechanisms.

## Management and Governance

**LLCs** offer maximum flexibility. There is no required board of directors, no mandatory annual meetings, and no statutory officer positions. You can structure your LLC as member-managed (all owners participate in decisions) or manager-managed (designated managers run the business while other members are passive investors). The operating agreement is a private document that can be customized to match virtually any management arrangement.

**Corporations** operate through a formal governance structure: shareholders elect a board of directors, the board appoints officers (president, secretary, treasurer), and the officers run daily operations. Annual shareholder and board meetings are required, and formal minutes must be maintained. This structure provides clear lines of authority, which is an advantage for larger organizations, but it imposes administrative overhead that many small businesses find unnecessary.

For most small business owners and entrepreneurs, the LLC's management flexibility is a significant advantage. You can run your business as you see fit without maintaining a corporate governance apparatus.

## Liability Protection

Both LLCs and Corporations provide limited liability, meaning your personal assets are generally protected from business debts and obligations. However, the mechanisms differ, and the level of protection is not identical in practice.

**LLCs** provide limited liability for members (owners). Creditors of the LLC can generally only reach the assets of the LLC itself, not the personal assets of the members. In states like Wyoming, where the charging order is the exclusive remedy for a creditor of an LLC member, the protection extends further: a judgment creditor of you personally (not the LLC) cannot seize your LLC interest, take over management, or force distributions. The creditor's only option is a charging order — a lien on future distributions. If the LLC does not make distributions, the creditor receives nothing while potentially owing taxes on allocated income (the "phantom income" problem under IRS Ruling 77-137).

**Corporations** provide limited liability for shareholders, but the asset protection framework is different. Corporate stock can be seized by a creditor — there is no equivalent of the charging order protection that LLCs enjoy. If you own shares in a corporation and a personal creditor obtains a judgment against you, the creditor may be able to seize those shares, gain voting rights, and potentially force changes in the corporation. This is a meaningful disadvantage compared to LLCs for asset protection purposes.

## Tax Treatment

**LLC default taxation:** A single-member LLC is treated as a "disregarded entity" for federal tax purposes — the IRS ignores the LLC and taxes all income on the owner's personal return. A multi-member LLC is taxed as a partnership by default. Under Treasury Regulation 301.7701-3, business entities that are not corporations can elect their tax classification. This means an LLC can choose to be taxed as a sole proprietorship, partnership, S-Corporation, or C-Corporation — giving the LLC maximum tax flexibility.

**Corporation default taxation:** A corporation is taxed as a C-Corporation by default, meaning the entity pays corporate income tax (21% federal rate) on its profits, and shareholders pay tax again on dividends received. This "double taxation" is the most frequently cited drawback of the C-Corp structure. A corporation can elect S-Corp taxation to avoid double taxation, but the election comes with restrictions (maximum 100 shareholders, only U.S. individual shareholders, one class of stock).

**Bottom line:** The LLC has superior tax flexibility because it can elect any tax treatment available under the Internal Revenue Code. A corporation can elect S-Corp taxation but cannot elect to be taxed as a disregarded entity or partnership.

## Asset Protection

For most small business owners, the LLC provides stronger asset protection than a corporation because of the charging order framework.

In Wyoming, if a creditor obtains a personal judgment against you and seeks to reach your LLC interest, the charging order is the exclusive remedy. The creditor cannot seize your membership interest, vote your interest, or force the LLC to make distributions. This protection applies to all Wyoming LLCs, including single-member LLCs.

Corporate stock does not receive this protection. A creditor can potentially seize corporate shares, exercise the voting rights associated with those shares, and participate in corporate decisions. For business owners who are forming an entity specifically for privacy and asset protection — which describes the majority of our clients — the LLC structure is preferable.

## Formality Requirements

**LLCs** have minimal statutory formality requirements. Wyoming does not require annual meetings, formal minutes, or a board of directors. That said, maintaining an operating agreement and keeping basic records is strongly recommended to support the entity's legal standing and prevent veil-piercing arguments.

**Corporations** must maintain formal corporate governance: annual shareholder meetings, board of directors meetings, written minutes of all meetings, proper documentation of board resolutions, and maintenance of a stock ledger. Failure to observe corporate formalities is one of the most common bases for piercing the corporate veil — allowing a plaintiff to reach through the corporation to the personal assets of shareholders.

## Our Recommendation

**For most small business owners, an LLC is the right choice.** It provides equivalent or stronger liability protection (with charging order advantages that corporations do not have), maximum management flexibility, superior tax options, and lower administrative overhead. The LLC is the default entity type for privacy-focused and asset-protection-focused formations for good reason.

**Choose a Corporation** if you plan to seek venture capital or institutional investment (most investors require C-Corp structure), if you plan to eventually go public, or if your specific industry requires a corporate structure for licensing or regulatory purposes.

When in doubt, form an LLC. You can always elect S-Corp or C-Corp taxation later without changing the underlying entity.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can an LLC be taxed as a Corporation?**
Yes. Under Treasury Regulation 301.7701-3, an LLC can elect to be taxed as a C-Corporation or S-Corporation by filing the appropriate forms with the IRS. The entity remains an LLC under state law with all of its flexibility and protections, but it is taxed as a corporation for federal purposes.

**What is "piercing the corporate veil"?**
This is when a court allows a plaintiff to reach through the entity's liability shield and hold the owners personally liable. It typically requires proving that the entity was not operated as a separate entity (commingled funds, no operating agreement, no separate accounts) or was used to commit fraud. Maintaining proper formalities — easier with an LLC — reduces this risk.

**Do I need a board of directors for an LLC?**
No. LLCs do not have boards of directors. They are managed by members (owners) or by designated managers, as defined in the operating agreement.

**Can I convert an LLC to a Corporation later?**
Yes. Most states allow entity conversion without dissolving and re-forming. Wyoming permits LLC-to-Corporation conversions through a statutory process.

**What is "phantom income" and how does it relate to LLCs?**
Under IRS Ruling 77-137, if a charging order is in place against an LLC membership interest, the creditor holding the charging order may be allocated taxable income from the LLC — even if no actual cash is distributed. This creates a negative incentive for creditors, making the charging order less attractive to pursue against LLCs that do not distribute all of their income.

**Which is better for holding real estate?**
LLCs are the standard choice for holding real estate. The charging order protection prevents a personal creditor from forcing a sale of the property through the LLC. Corporations do not provide this protection.

**Which is better for a solo entrepreneur?**
An LLC. Single-member LLCs in Wyoming receive full charging order protection, and the minimal formality requirements make an LLC easier and cheaper to maintain than a corporation for a one-person business.

**What if my business partner wants a Corporation?**
Discuss the specific reasons. If they want corporate taxation, an LLC can elect S-Corp or C-Corp treatment while retaining LLC flexibility. If they want investor-ready structure, a C-Corp may be appropriate. In most cases, an LLC with the right tax election achieves the same goal with more flexibility.

**Can I have both an LLC and a Corporation?**
Yes. Some business structures use a holding LLC that owns a subsidiary corporation, or vice versa. This can be useful for separating operating liability from asset holding. Contact us to discuss whether a multi-entity structure is appropriate for your situation.

**Do nominee services work the same for LLCs and Corporations?**
Yes. Our nominees serve as managers (LLC) or officers and directors (Corporation) on all public filings. The privacy protection is equivalent for both entity types.
