# Asset Protection

You have spent years building wealth. A single lawsuit can threaten everything you own — your investment accounts, rental properties, business interests, and personal savings. In the United States, approximately 40 million lawsuits are filed each year. Many target individuals who have done nothing wrong but happen to have assets worth pursuing.

Asset protection is not about hiding wealth or avoiding legitimate obligations. It is the legal practice of restructuring how you hold assets so that a judgment creditor cannot simply seize them. When done correctly and proactively, asset protection uses the same statutes and legal structures that courts have upheld for decades.

At Incorporate123, we have spent 25 years helping business owners and investors implement proven asset protection strategies using Wyoming and Nevada LLCs. Our approach is straightforward: we help you place assets into legal structures that create barriers between your personal wealth and potential creditors, all within the bounds of state and federal law.

## How Asset Protection Works

Asset protection rests on a simple legal principle: a creditor can only collect against assets you personally own. When your assets are held by a properly structured LLC, you no longer own those assets directly. Instead, you own a membership interest in the LLC — and that membership interest is protected by charging order statutes.

Here is how the mechanism works in practice:

A creditor wins a judgment against you personally. They conduct an asset search and discover that your bank accounts, investment portfolios, and real estate are not held in your name. They are held by one or more LLCs. The creditor cannot simply seize those assets. Under the [charging order protection](/asset-protection/charging-order-protection/) statutes in Wyoming and Nevada, the creditor's only remedy is to obtain a charging order — essentially a lien on any distributions the LLC makes to you. The creditor cannot force the LLC to make distributions, cannot vote on LLC matters, and cannot access LLC assets directly.

This creates a powerful deterrent. Most creditors and their attorneys, upon discovering that your assets are held in a well-structured LLC in Wyoming or Nevada, will recognize that collection will be extremely difficult, time-consuming, and expensive. The result is often a significantly reduced settlement or an abandoned claim.

The strength of your asset protection depends on two factors: the state where your LLC is formed and how the structure is implemented. That is why we focus exclusively on Wyoming and Nevada — the two states with the strongest charging order protections in the country.

Nevada's asset protection statutes, particularly NRS 86.401, provide that a charging order is the exclusive remedy available to a judgment creditor. Our Nevada structures are ideal for clients who already operate businesses in Nevada or who want the added benefit of Nevada's business-friendly court system.

### [Wyoming Asset Protection](/asset-protection/wyoming-asset-protection/)

Wyoming offers what many attorneys consider the strongest LLC protection available in any U.S. state. Wyoming explicitly extends charging order protection to single-member LLCs under Wyoming Statute 17-29-503 — a critical advantage that most states do not provide. Combined with Wyoming's low costs ($62 annual report, no state income tax) and strong privacy protections, Wyoming is our most recommended jurisdiction for asset protection.

### [Investment Holding LLC](/asset-protection/investment-holding-llc/)

An investment holding LLC is a dedicated entity that holds and manages assets — cash, securities, real estate, intellectual property — without conducting active business operations. This separation is essential: the LLC that holds your assets should never sign contracts, employ staff, or engage in activities that could create liability. We help you structure and fund these entities correctly.

### [Charging Order Protection](/asset-protection/charging-order-protection/)

The charging order is the legal mechanism that makes LLC-based asset protection work. Understanding how charging orders function in Wyoming (Statute 17-29-503) and Nevada (NRS 86.401) is essential to understanding why these structures are effective. We explain the statutes, the relevant case law, and how these protections apply to your specific situation.

### [Real Estate Asset Protection](/asset-protection/real-estate-asset-protection/)

Investment real estate creates significant liability exposure. A single slip-and-fall claim on a rental property can put your entire portfolio at risk if your properties are held in your personal name. We help real estate investors structure their holdings using multi-entity LLC strategies that isolate liability and protect equity across their portfolio.

### [LLC vs. Living Trust](/asset-protection/llc-vs-living-trust/)

Many people confuse asset protection with estate planning. A living trust helps your family avoid probate and manages asset transfer after death, but it provides zero protection from lawsuits during your lifetime. An LLC provides creditor protection while you are alive. In many cases, the optimal strategy uses both — with the LLC owned by the trust.

## Who Needs Asset Protection?

Asset protection is not only for the ultra-wealthy. We generally recommend formal asset protection structures for anyone with $500,000 or more in personal assets. That includes:

- **Real estate investors** holding rental properties, commercial buildings, or vacant land
- **Business owners** whose personal assets could be exposed through business-related lawsuits
- **Professionals** in high-liability fields — physicians, contractors, consultants, real estate agents
- **Retirees and pre-retirees** who have accumulated savings and investment portfolios over a lifetime
- **Entrepreneurs** launching new ventures who want to separate personal wealth from business risk
- **Residents of community property states** (Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin) where a spouse's liability can put jointly held assets at risk

The common thread is straightforward: if you have assets worth protecting and you face any potential liability exposure — even theoretical — proactive planning is significantly more effective than reactive scrambling after a lawsuit is filed.

## The Connection Between Privacy and Asset Protection

Privacy and asset protection work together. The first line of defense in any asset protection strategy is ensuring that your name does not appear in public records connected to your assets.

When a plaintiff's attorney evaluates whether to pursue a claim, one of the first steps is an asset search — checking public records for property, business filings, and financial accounts tied to your name and Social Security number. If that search reveals little or nothing, you become a far less attractive target.

Both Wyoming and Nevada allow LLCs to be formed without listing member names in public filings. Our [nominee services](/privacy/) go further, removing your name from officer and manager records as well. This means a standard asset search will not connect your name to the assets held within your LLC.

Privacy is not a substitute for proper legal structure — but combined with charging order protection and correct entity formation, it creates a comprehensive shield that deters most potential plaintiffs before litigation even begins.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is asset protection legal?**
Yes. Asset protection uses legitimate legal structures — LLCs, trusts, and corporate entities — that are recognized and governed by state statutes. Courts routinely uphold properly structured asset protection plans. The key requirement is that structures must be established before any claim arises. Transferring assets after a lawsuit is filed can be challenged as a fraudulent conveyance.

**When should I set up asset protection?**
The best time is before you need it. Asset protection structures must be in place before a claim or lawsuit arises to be fully effective. Once you are aware of a potential claim, transferring assets can be challenged under fraudulent transfer statutes. We recommend establishing your structure as soon as your personal assets exceed $500,000.

**Do I lose control of my assets?**
No. You maintain full control of your assets through your LLC membership interest and your role as outlined in the operating agreement. The assets are managed by the LLC, and you direct the LLC's activities. The legal ownership shifts to the entity, but your practical control remains unchanged.

**Which state is better for asset protection — Wyoming or Nevada?**
Both states offer excellent protection. Wyoming has a slight edge for most clients because it explicitly protects single-member LLCs (important if you are the sole owner), has lower annual fees ($62 annual report vs. Nevada's $350 business license fee), and provides the same level of privacy. Nevada is preferred when you already have business operations in the state or want access to Nevada's specialized business court.

**Can a creditor force my LLC to make distributions?**
No. Under both Wyoming Statute 17-29-503 and Nevada's NRS 86.401, a charging order is the exclusive remedy. The creditor receives only a lien on distributions — they cannot force the LLC to distribute anything, cannot vote on LLC matters, and cannot access LLC assets or records.

**What assets can I protect with an LLC?**
Cash and bank accounts, investment securities, real estate (including your personal residence in many cases), intellectual property, notes and receivables, vehicles, and interests in other businesses can all be held within an LLC for asset protection purposes.
