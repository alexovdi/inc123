# Wyoming vs Delaware for LLC Formation

Delaware has been the default state for corporate formation for decades, and its reputation is well-earned — the Court of Chancery, the extensive body of corporate case law, and the business-friendly legislative environment make it the gold standard for large corporations. But for small business LLCs, the calculus is different. Wyoming offers stronger asset protection, better privacy, and significantly lower costs for the entity type that most small business owners actually use. This comparison explains when each state is the right choice.

## The Single-Member LLC Problem in Delaware

This is the most important difference for the typical small business owner, and it is the reason we recommend Wyoming over Delaware for most of our clients.

Wyoming Statute 17-29-503 establishes the charging order as the exclusive remedy for a judgment creditor of any LLC member — including single-member LLCs. A creditor cannot seize your membership interest, take over management, or force the LLC to distribute assets. The creditor is limited to a charging order (a lien on future distributions), which creates a strong deterrent because the creditor may owe taxes on income allocated to the charged interest even if no cash is distributed.

Delaware does not provide this protection for single-member LLCs. While Delaware's LLC Act provides charging order protection for multi-member LLCs, courts have held that when an LLC has only one member, a creditor may be able to reach the membership interest directly — because there are no other members whose interests would be harmed by a more aggressive remedy. This creates a critical gap: the entity type most commonly used by solo entrepreneurs (single-member LLC) receives little to no charging order protection in Delaware.

For multi-member LLCs, Delaware's charging order protection is solid. But most small business owners form single-member LLCs, and for them, Delaware's protection is effectively absent.

## Privacy Comparison

Both Wyoming and Delaware have reasonable baseline privacy for LLCs. Neither state requires disclosure of members or managers on formation documents, which means basic ownership information is not part of the public record in either state.

## When Delaware Makes Sense

Delaware is the right choice in specific, well-defined circumstances:

**Venture capital or institutional investment.** Most VC firms require a Delaware C-Corporation because of the established case law, the Court of Chancery, and the familiarity of their legal teams with Delaware corporate law. If you are seeking outside investment, Delaware C-Corp may be a requirement rather than a choice.

**Planning to go public.** The vast majority of publicly traded companies are incorporated in Delaware. If an IPO is part of your long-term plan, starting in Delaware avoids a future domestication.

**Complex corporate governance.** If your business involves multiple classes of stock, complex shareholder agreements, or governance structures that benefit from Delaware's extensive case law on corporate governance, Delaware's legal environment is genuinely superior.

**Multi-member LLC with sophisticated operating agreement.** Delaware's LLC Act (the Delaware Limited Liability Company Act, 6 Del. C. Chapter 18) is extremely flexible and allows for highly customized operating agreements. For complex multi-member structures, Delaware's permissive approach has significant advantages.

## When Wyoming Is the Better Choice

Wyoming is superior for the typical small business owner in nearly every scenario that does not involve the specific Delaware advantages listed above.

**Solo entrepreneurs forming single-member LLCs.** Wyoming's explicit statutory protection for single-member LLCs is the decisive factor. Delaware does not provide equivalent protection.

**Asset protection.** Wyoming's one-year fraudulent transfer lookback (vs. Delaware's four years) and its exclusive charging order remedy for all LLCs make it the stronger asset protection state.

**Cost-conscious formations.** Wyoming's annual costs are $240 less per year than Delaware's, with no franchise tax at any level. This savings compounds over the life of the entity.

**Privacy-focused formations.** Wyoming's infrastructure for privacy services (nominees, offshore records, privacy-focused registered agents) is more developed for the small business market than Delaware's.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is Delaware really the "best state" for business?**
For large corporations, publicly traded companies, and venture-backed startups — yes, Delaware's Court of Chancery and case law provide genuine advantages. For small business LLCs, those advantages do not apply, and Wyoming provides stronger protections at lower cost.

**What is the Court of Chancery?**
Delaware's Court of Chancery is a specialized business court that handles corporate disputes without juries. It is staffed by judges with deep expertise in corporate law. This is a significant advantage for complex corporate litigation but is rarely relevant to small business LLC disputes.

**Can I form in Wyoming and get investment from Delaware-focused VCs?**
Possibly, but most VCs will require you to domesticate (convert) to a Delaware C-Corporation as a condition of investment. If you anticipate seeking VC funding, discuss this with your attorney before choosing a formation state.

**Why is Delaware's franchise tax so much higher?**
Delaware relies heavily on entity fees as a state revenue source. The $300 LLC franchise tax and the much higher corporate franchise tax (which can reach tens of thousands for large corporations) are a deliberate policy choice. Wyoming chose a different model with minimal entity fees.

**Does Wyoming's LLC statute hold up in court?**
Yes. Wyoming created the LLC in 1977 and has nearly five decades of case law. The state's courts have consistently upheld charging order protections, respected operating agreement terms, and maintained business-friendly interpretations.

**What if I need both states?**
You can form in Wyoming and file a foreign registration in Delaware (or vice versa) if you need a presence in both states. For most small businesses, a Wyoming home entity with foreign registrations as needed is the most cost-effective and protective approach.

**Can I convert a Delaware LLC to a Wyoming LLC?**
Yes. Most states allow domestication — the process of converting an entity from one state to another without dissolving and re-forming. We can assist with domestication from Delaware to Wyoming if you determine Wyoming better serves your needs.

**Is Delaware's Court of Chancery relevant for LLC disputes?**
The Court of Chancery handles all business entity disputes, including LLC cases. However, most small business LLC disputes do not require the specialized expertise that makes the Court of Chancery valuable for complex corporate governance matters. For straightforward LLC issues, Wyoming's courts are well-equipped and have nearly five decades of LLC-specific case law.

**Does Delaware have nominee services like Wyoming?**
Delaware has registered agent services, but the state's formation ecosystem is not oriented toward privacy-focused small business formations the way Wyoming's is. Finding comprehensive nominee services, offshore record storage, and privacy-focused compliance management in Delaware is more difficult and typically more expensive.

**What about the Beneficial Ownership Information (BOI) reporting requirement?**
The Corporate Transparency Act requires most LLCs and corporations to report beneficial ownership information to FinCEN regardless of the state of formation. This is a federal requirement that applies equally to Wyoming, Delaware, Nevada, and all other states. It does not affect the privacy advantages of forming in a specific state, as the BOI report is not publicly accessible.
