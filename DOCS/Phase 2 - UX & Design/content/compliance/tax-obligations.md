# Tax Filing & Obligations

Forming an LLC or corporation creates tax obligations that did not exist when you were operating as a sole proprietor or had no formal business entity. These obligations vary significantly depending on your state of formation, where you operate, how your entity is taxed, and what elections you have made with the IRS.

Understanding these obligations upfront prevents costly surprises. Too many business owners form an entity and then discover months or years later that they owe back taxes, franchise fees, or penalties they never knew about.

### Wyoming

Wyoming is one of the most tax-friendly states in the country for business entities:

- **No state income tax** — Wyoming does not impose any income tax on individuals or businesses.
- **No franchise tax** — There is no franchise tax or privilege tax for LLCs or corporations.
- **Annual report fee:** $62/year — This is technically an annual report filing fee, not a tax, but it is the only recurring state-level cost for maintaining your entity.
- **No gross receipts tax** — Wyoming does not impose a gross receipts or commercial activity tax.

For entities that do not operate within Wyoming (for example, a Wyoming LLC used as a holding company or for privacy and asset protection), the annual report fee of $62 is often the only state-level cost.

### Nevada

Nevada is frequently marketed as a "no tax" state, and while that is partially true, there are important costs to understand:

- **No state income tax** — Nevada does not impose a state income tax on individuals or entities.
- **No franchise tax** — There is no traditional franchise tax.
- **State Business License:** $200/year for LLCs, $500/year for corporations — This is required for every entity and is renewed annually with the Secretary of State.
- **Annual List filing fee:** $150/year for LLCs, $150+/year for corporations — Required annually to maintain good standing.
- **Commerce Tax** — Entities with Nevada-sourced gross revenue exceeding $4 million per year are subject to the Commerce Tax (rates vary by industry). Most small businesses are exempt from this.
- **Modified Business Tax** — Employers in Nevada pay a modified business tax on quarterly wages paid to employees. The rate depends on whether the entity provides qualifying health benefits.

The combined annual state fees for a Nevada LLC ($150 annual list + $200 business license = $350 minimum) are higher than Wyoming's $62, but Nevada's other advantages — including privacy protections and no income tax — make it a strong choice for many business structures.

### California

California is the most expensive state for LLC maintenance and has the most aggressive approach to taxing business entities:

- **Franchise Tax:** $800/year minimum for every LLC — This applies regardless of whether the LLC earns any revenue. It is due by the 15th day of the 4th month after the beginning of the tax year.
- **LLC Annual Fee** — LLCs with California-sourced gross income over $250,000 pay an additional annual fee ranging from $900 to $11,790, based on income brackets.
- **State income tax** — California imposes a state income tax on business income earned in the state. For corporations, the rate is 8.84%. For S-Corps, there is a 1.5% tax on net income (minimum $800).
- **Statement of Information:** $20 every two years — A relatively minor cost compared to the franchise tax.

The $800 minimum franchise tax is the single most important number to understand about California LLCs. It applies to every LLC "doing business" in California, and the state defines "doing business" very broadly — including having a member who is a California resident. Even a Wyoming LLC with a California-resident member may owe this tax.

### Florida

Florida has a moderate tax environment for business entities:

- **No state income tax on individuals** — Florida does not tax individual income, which benefits sole proprietors and pass-through entities.
- **Corporate income tax:** 5.5% on income over $50,000 — This applies to C-Corporations. Pass-through entities (S-Corps, LLCs taxed as partnerships) are generally not subject to this.
- **Annual report fee:** $138.75/year — Due May 1 every year, with a $400 late fee.
- **No franchise tax** — Florida eliminated its franchise tax on corporations.
- **Reemployment tax** — Florida's version of unemployment tax, applicable if you have employees.

Florida is a solid middle ground — lower annual costs than Nevada, no individual income tax, and a straightforward annual report process.

## Federal Tax Considerations

Your entity's state of formation determines your state tax obligations, but your federal tax treatment is determined by how you elect to be taxed with the IRS:

### Single-Member LLC (Default: Disregarded Entity)

The IRS treats a single-member LLC as a "disregarded entity" by default. All income and expenses flow through to your personal tax return (Schedule C). The LLC itself does not file a separate federal return, though it may need an EIN for banking and employment purposes.

### Multi-Member LLC (Default: Partnership)

A multi-member LLC is taxed as a partnership by default. The LLC files an informational return (Form 1065), and each member receives a Schedule K-1 reporting their share of income and deductions.

### S-Corporation Election

Any LLC or corporation can elect to be taxed as an S-Corp by filing Form 2553 with the IRS. S-Corp taxation can provide significant tax savings on self-employment taxes for certain businesses, but it comes with additional requirements — including reasonable salary for owner-employees, quarterly payroll filings, and maintaining proper corporate records to support the election.

### C-Corporation

Corporations are taxed as C-Corps by default. C-Corps pay corporate income tax on profits, and shareholders pay personal income tax on dividends — the so-called "double taxation." Some businesses benefit from C-Corp status due to the 21% corporate tax rate and the ability to retain earnings in the entity.

## Common Tax Mistakes After Formation

### Not Paying California's Franchise Tax

Many business owners form a Wyoming or Nevada LLC and assume they have no California tax obligations. If the LLC has any connection to California — a California-resident member, California customers, or California-sourced income — the $800 franchise tax may apply.

### Missing the First-Year Deadline

Some states impose tax obligations almost immediately after formation. Nevada's business license fee is due with the Initial List filing — within the first month or two of formation. California's franchise tax for the first year has specific timing rules that differ from subsequent years.

### Not Making Quarterly Estimated Payments

If your entity earns income, you may be required to make quarterly estimated tax payments at both the state and federal level. Failing to make estimated payments can result in underpayment penalties.

### Ignoring Nexus in Multiple States

If your entity has "nexus" (a sufficient connection) in multiple states, you may owe taxes in each of those states. This is increasingly common for e-commerce businesses. Each state where you have nexus may require a foreign registration and comes with its own tax obligations.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does Wyoming charge any taxes on LLCs?

No. Wyoming has no state income tax, no franchise tax, and no gross receipts tax. The only recurring state-level cost is the $62 annual report filing fee.

### Does Nevada charge any taxes on LLCs?

Nevada has no state income tax or franchise tax, but every LLC must pay $200/year for the state business license and $150/year for the annual list filing. Entities with over $4 million in Nevada gross revenue may also owe the Commerce Tax.

### Do I owe California franchise tax if my LLC is formed in Wyoming?

Possibly. California imposes the $800 franchise tax on any LLC "doing business" in California. Having a California-resident member, earning California-sourced income, or maintaining property in California can trigger this obligation even for out-of-state LLCs.

### What is the difference between a franchise tax and an income tax?

An income tax is based on the entity's profits or net income. A franchise tax is a fee for the privilege of doing business in a state, often calculated regardless of income. California's $800 LLC franchise tax applies even if the LLC earns zero revenue.

### Do I need to file a federal tax return for my LLC?

It depends on how the LLC is taxed. A single-member LLC (disregarded entity) reports on the owner's personal return. A multi-member LLC files Form 1065. An LLC electing S-Corp treatment files Form 1120-S. A C-Corp files Form 1120.

### What is nexus, and why does it matter?

Nexus is the legal term for having a sufficient connection to a state to trigger tax and registration obligations. Nexus can be created by having employees, property, inventory, or significant sales activity in a state. Each state with nexus may require foreign registration and tax payments.

### When do I need to start paying estimated taxes?

Generally, if you expect to owe $1,000 or more in federal tax for the year, you should make quarterly estimated payments (due April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15). State estimated payment requirements vary. Your CPA can advise on your specific situation.

### What is the Commerce Tax in Nevada?

The Commerce Tax applies to entities with Nevada-sourced gross revenue exceeding $4 million per year. Rates vary by industry. Most small businesses and entities that do not operate in Nevada are not subject to this tax.

### Does Florida have an income tax for LLCs?

Florida does not tax individual income, and pass-through entities (LLCs taxed as partnerships or S-Corps) generally are not subject to Florida income tax. C-Corporations pay a 5.5% tax on income over $50,000.

### What happens if I do not pay the California franchise tax?

The Franchise Tax Board will assess penalties and interest. Continued non-payment can result in the LLC being suspended — meaning it cannot conduct business, file lawsuits, or defend lawsuits in California courts. Reinstatement requires paying all back taxes, penalties, and interest.

### Should I elect S-Corp taxation for my LLC?

S-Corp election can save money on self-employment taxes if your business earns enough to justify paying yourself a reasonable salary. However, it adds compliance requirements — payroll, quarterly filings, and proper corporate records. This decision should be made with your CPA based on your specific income level and business structure.
