# Nevada Asset Protection

If you own assets worth protecting — investment accounts, real estate, intellectual property, or business interests — you face a straightforward problem. Anyone can file a lawsuit against you, and if they win a judgment, they can pursue everything you own personally. Insurance may cover some claims, but policies have limits, exclusions, and carriers who employ teams of attorneys to find reasons to deny coverage.

Nevada has addressed this problem more directly than almost any other state. Through a combination of strong LLC statutes, charging order protection under NRS 86.401, a dedicated business court, and no state income tax, Nevada offers one of the most robust legal environments for personal asset protection in the United States. For 25 years, Incorporate123 has helped clients use these Nevada statutes to build structures that put meaningful legal barriers between their personal wealth and potential creditors.

## How Nevada Asset Protection Works

The foundation of Nevada asset protection is the Nevada Limited Liability Company. Unlike in 14 other states — including California, New York, Texas, and Virginia — Nevada does not require an LLC to have a business purpose. A Nevada LLC can be formed specifically to hold and manage personal assets as an investment holding company.

Here is the core mechanism: You transfer personal assets (cash, securities, real estate, intellectual property, receivables) into a Nevada LLC. In exchange, you receive a membership interest in the LLC. You no longer own those assets personally — the LLC does. You maintain full control of the assets through the LLC's operating agreement, but the legal ownership belongs to the entity.

When a creditor obtains a judgment against you personally, they discover that the assets they want to collect are not yours. They belong to the LLC. Under NRS 86.401, the creditor's only available remedy is a charging order — a court order that places a lien on any distributions the LLC makes to its members. Critically, the statute specifies that a charging order is the **exclusive remedy**. The creditor cannot:

- Force the LLC to make distributions
- Seize assets held within the LLC
- Vote on LLC matters or interfere with management
- Gain access to LLC records or accounts
- Foreclose on your membership interest

This makes the creditor's position uncomfortable. They hold a lien on distributions that may never come, and depending on the LLC's tax election, they may even face tax liability on income allocated to them through the charging order — a situation known as "phantom income." This dynamic gives you significant leverage in any settlement negotiation.

## Nevada's Additional Legal Advantages

### Strongest Corporate Veil in the Country

Nevada is widely recognized as the most difficult state in which to "pierce the corporate veil." In the landmark case *Rowland v. LePire* (99 Nev. 308, 1983), the Nevada Supreme Court refused to pierce the corporate veil even when the corporation was undercapitalized, held no formal meetings, paid no dividends, and maintained no minute book. The court held that these deficiencies alone were not enough — a plaintiff must also prove fraud or injustice that directly caused their injury.

In *Polaris Indus. Corp. v. Kaplan* (103 Nev. 598, 1987), the court pierced the veil only because the owner directly withdrew corporate funds that would have otherwise paid the debt. This remains the sole instance of veil piercing in Nevada in over 40 years, and it required outright fraud.

This protection applies to Nevada entities even when sued in other states. A California judge hearing a case against a Nevada LLC must apply Nevada law when evaluating whether to pierce the entity, giving your structure protection regardless of where litigation occurs.

### No Joint and Several Liability

Nevada has abolished joint and several liability. If multiple defendants are found liable in a lawsuit, each pays only their percentage of fault — not the full judgment amount. This prevents the common plaintiff strategy of naming a wealthy defendant alongside less solvent parties to collect the full judgment from the deepest pockets.

### Dedicated Business Court

Nevada operates a specialized business court that handles complex entity and commercial disputes. Judges in this court have specific expertise in business law, which means your LLC structure is evaluated by jurists who understand and routinely apply the state's protective statutes.

### No State Income Tax

Nevada imposes no personal or corporate income tax. Assets held within your Nevada LLC grow without state tax liability, making Nevada particularly attractive for holding investment portfolios, rental income, and other income-producing assets.

### Confidentiality Protections

Under NRS 76.160, the records and files of the Secretary of State concerning business administration are confidential and privileged. Combined with our nominee services — which remove your name from all public filings — your Nevada LLC creates a strong privacy barrier that deters asset searches before they begin.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is NRS 86.401?**
NRS 86.401 is the Nevada statute that establishes charging order protection for LLC members. It states that a charging order is the exclusive remedy by which a judgment creditor can attempt to satisfy a judgment from a member's LLC interest. The creditor receives only a lien on distributions and cannot seize LLC assets, force distributions, or interfere with management.

**Can I use a Nevada LLC for asset protection if I live in another state?**
Yes. You can form a Nevada LLC regardless of where you live. The LLC is governed by Nevada law, which means Nevada's protective statutes apply to your entity. However, depending on your state, you may need to register the LLC as a foreign entity if it conducts business locally. We advise on these requirements during your consultation.

**How is a Nevada LLC different from a corporation for asset protection?**
An LLC provides superior asset protection because of the charging order statute. Corporate shares can potentially be seized by a creditor through a writ of execution. LLC membership interests are protected — the creditor can only obtain a charging order, which is a much weaker remedy. This is why we recommend LLCs over corporations for asset protection purposes.

**What is "phantom income" and why does it matter?**
If a charging order is issued against your LLC membership interest, the creditor may be allocated a share of the LLC's taxable income — even if no cash is actually distributed to them. This means the creditor could owe taxes on income they never received. This creates additional pressure on the creditor to settle or abandon the claim.

**Does Nevada protect single-member LLCs?**
Nevada's statute is less explicit than Wyoming's on this point. While NRS 86.401 states that a charging order is the exclusive remedy, some legal commentators note that Nevada has not addressed single-member LLCs as directly as Wyoming (which explicitly includes sole members in its statute). For clients who will be the sole LLC member, we often recommend Wyoming as the formation state.

**What assets can I put in a Nevada LLC?**
Cash, bank accounts, investment securities, notes, receivables, investment real estate, personal residences (in many cases), patents, trademarks, copyrights, and shares in closely held corporations are all eligible. The key rule: the holding LLC should not conduct active business operations that could create liability.

**How long does formation take?**
Standard formation takes 3-5 business days. Expedited filing with the Nevada Secretary of State is available for an additional fee and can be completed in 24 hours.

**Can I set up asset protection after I have been sued?**
Transferring assets after a lawsuit has been filed — or after you become aware of a likely claim — can be challenged as a fraudulent conveyance. Asset protection is most effective when structures are established well before any legal action. This is why we strongly recommend acting proactively.

**What is the difference between Nevada and Wyoming for asset protection?**
Both states offer excellent protection. Nevada's advantages include a dedicated business court, extensive case law supporting entity protections, and the strongest corporate veil in the country. Wyoming's advantages include explicit single-member LLC protection, lower annual fees ($62 vs. approximately $350), and equally strong privacy. We help you choose based on your specific situation.
