# Competitive Website Analysis — Deep Research Prompt

## How to Use This Prompt

This prompt is designed for Claude's **Deep Research** feature. It covers all three analysis dimensions (site structure, SEO positioning, package presentation) across the five target competitors. Due to the depth required, you should run this as **three separate research sessions** — one per dimension — rather than one massive prompt. Each section below is self-contained and ready to copy-paste.

---

## Research Session 1: Site Structure & Information Architecture

```
I'm conducting a competitive website analysis for a website redesign project. I need you to do a deep, thorough analysis of site structure and information architecture for 5 competitors in the business formation / privacy incorporation space.

## Context

My client is Incorporate123 (incorporate123.co), a 25-year-old company specializing in privacy-focused business formation, nominee services, and asset protection in Nevada and Wyoming. We're redesigning their website, repositioning from "Nevada incorporation company" to "Privacy, Asset Protection & Business Formation Specialists." The new site will use an intent-first pillar/cluster architecture with 4 pillars: Business Privacy, Asset Protection, Company Formation, and Compliance — organized around ~71 pages.

## The 5 Competitors to Analyze

1. **NCH Inc** — https://nchinc.com (also nvinc.com) — Big Nevada incorporator, high-volume, 30+ years, 11,989 Trustpilot reviews. Known for business credit coaching and $100,000 corporate veil guarantee. No nominee services.
2. **Anderson Advisors** — https://andersonadvisors.com — Attorney-led full-service operation. Nominee services, asset protection, tax planning. Premium positioning with free "strategy session" lead gen.
3. **ShieldCorp** — https://shieldcorp.net — Nominee services provider in Nevada and Wyoming. Likely offers "nominee for a day" only (does NOT maintain corporate records/minutes). Smaller operation.
4. **Wyoming Corporate Services** — https://wyomingcompany.com — Closest direct competitor. Wyoming-only focus. Attorney-privilege packages ($1,765–$2,700). 5.0 Trustpilot with 1,271 reviews. BBB A+. Accepts Bitcoin.
5. **ZenBusiness** — https://zenbusiness.com — Budget benchmark. $0 formation + state fees. All 50 states. 25,821 Trustpilot reviews. No nominee services, no privacy features. Included to understand the mainstream UX standard clients compare against.

## What I Need Analyzed Per Competitor

For each of the 5 competitors, research and document:

### Navigation & Information Architecture
- Primary navigation structure (main menu items and their dropdowns/mega menus)
- How the site categorizes services — is it organized by state, by entity type (LLC, corp, trust), by use case (privacy, asset protection, tax), or some hybrid?
- Secondary navigation (footer, sidebar, breadcrumbs)
- Total estimated page count (use site:domain.com in Google to estimate indexed pages)
- Maximum click depth from homepage to deepest content
- Whether they have a blog/resource center/learning center, and roughly how many posts

### Content Architecture
- Ratio of transactional/service pages vs. educational/content pages
- State-specific page structure (do they have pages per state? How many states?)
- How privacy and asset protection topics are organized (if at all)
- Whether they use a pillar/cluster content model or flat structure
- FAQ implementation (dedicated FAQ page, inline on service pages, or both)
- How packages/pricing are organized within the navigation

### Conversion Architecture
- Number of clicks from homepage to "start your order" or equivalent CTA
- CTA placement patterns (header, hero, inline, sticky)
- Primary CTA copy (exact wording: "Start an LLC", "Order Now", "Book Consultation", etc.)
- Lead capture mechanisms (forms, chatbots, phone, free consultations)
- Whether the site supports self-service ordering or requires consultation first

### Technology
- CMS platform (WordPress, custom, Squarespace, etc.)
- Key technology stack clues (checkout system, form tools, chat widgets)
- Mobile responsiveness quality (basic assessment)

## Deliverable Format

Present findings in a structured comparison table where possible. For each competitor, provide:
1. A 3-4 sentence architecture summary
2. Detailed breakdown using the categories above
3. Notable strengths and weaknesses of their site structure specifically

Then provide a cross-competitor synthesis:
- Common patterns across all 5 (what everyone does)
- Unique approaches (what only 1-2 competitors do)
- Universal gaps (what NO competitor does well)
- Specific implications for Incorporate123's redesign

Be thorough and specific — cite actual page names, URL paths, and navigation labels you find. I need real data, not generalizations.
```

## Research Session 2: SEO Positioning & Content Strategy

```
I'm conducting a competitive SEO analysis for a website redesign project in the business formation / privacy incorporation space. I need deep research on SEO positioning, content strategy, and keyword landscape across 5 competitors.

## Context

My client is Incorporate123 (incorporate123.co), repositioning from "Nevada incorporation company" to "Privacy, Asset Protection & Business Formation Specialists." The new site targets privacy-conscious entrepreneurs and high-net-worth individuals ($500k+ assets) seeking anonymous LLCs, nominee services, offshore structures, and asset protection — NOT generic LLC formation.

We already know from preliminary research:
- Budget competitors (ZenBusiness, LegalZoom, Bizee) dominate generic keywords like "LLC formation" (30k+ monthly volume), "form an LLC" (50k+), "start an LLC" (40k+) — we will NOT compete for these.
- The opportunity is in privacy/protection niche keywords: "anonymous LLC formation" (720–1,100/mo), "best state for LLC privacy" (390–590), "nominee director service" (260–480), "hide LLC ownership" (200–400), "Wyoming LLC privacy" (140–210).
- Content topics where NO competitor has authority: anonymous LLC state-by-state comparisons, nominee services education, Wyoming vs Nevada from privacy perspective, Corporate Transparency Act impact on LLC privacy, privacy strategies for specific professions.

## The 5 Competitors

1. **NCH Inc** — nchinc.com
2. **Anderson Advisors** — andersonadvisors.com
3. **ShieldCorp** — shieldcorp.net
4. **Wyoming Corporate Services** — wyomingcompany.com
5. **ZenBusiness** — zenbusiness.com (budget benchmark)

## What I Need Researched

### Domain Authority & Backlink Landscape
- Estimated domain authority/rating for each competitor
- Approximate number of referring domains
- Quality indicators — do they get links from authoritative legal, business, or financial publications?
- Any notable link-building strategies visible (guest posts, resource pages, PR mentions, legal citations)

### Keyword Positioning
For each competitor, identify:
- What privacy/asset protection keywords they rank for (if any)
- What their top organic keywords appear to be based on their content
- Whether they target long-tail privacy keywords or stick to generic formation terms
- How they handle state-specific keyword targeting (e.g., "Wyoming LLC" vs "form LLC in Wyoming" vs "Wyoming LLC privacy")

### Content Strategy Analysis
For each competitor, evaluate:
- Blog/content publishing frequency and volume
- Content quality and depth (thin 300-word posts vs. comprehensive 2,000+ word guides)
- E-E-A-T signals: Do they display author credentials? Attorney review badges? Expert citations? Case law references?
- Content topics covered — specifically, do they create content about:
  - Anonymous LLC formation
  - Nominee services (what they are, how they work)
  - Asset protection strategies
  - State privacy comparisons
  - Corporate Transparency Act / BOI reporting
  - Offshore structures
  - Privacy for specific professions
- Content formats used (guides, comparison tables, calculators, videos, infographics, downloadable resources)
- Internal linking sophistication (do they link between related content, or is the blog disconnected from service pages?)

### SERP Feature Ownership
For these specific search queries, research who currently owns key SERP positions:
- "anonymous LLC"
- "anonymous LLC formation"
- "nominee director services"
- "best state for LLC privacy"
- "asset protection LLC"
- "Wyoming LLC privacy"
- "hide LLC ownership"
- "nominee for a day"
- "offshore asset protection"
- "Corporate Transparency Act LLC"

For each query, note: who appears in top 3 organic results, whether there's a featured snippet (and who owns it), People Also Ask questions, and whether AI Overviews appear.

### Technical SEO Signals
For each competitor:
- URL structure patterns (clean/messy, keyword usage, folder depth)
- Schema markup presence (Organization, Service, FAQ, HowTo, BreadcrumbList)
- Core Web Vitals — run each competitor's homepage through the lens of load speed, mobile-friendliness
- Meta title/description patterns for key service pages
- Sitemap availability and structure (XML sitemap)

### Content Gap Opportunities
Based on all the above, identify:
- High-value topics NO competitor covers comprehensively
- Topics where existing competitor content is thin, outdated, or low-quality — beatable with better content
- Long-tail keyword clusters with commercial intent that are underserved
- Specific content types/formats no competitor is using that could differentiate (interactive tools, comparison calculators, state privacy scorecards, etc.)

## Deliverable Format

1. **Per-competitor SEO profiles** (domain metrics, content strategy summary, keyword focus, strengths/weaknesses)
2. **Keyword landscape map** — organized by intent cluster (privacy, asset protection, formation, compliance, comparison)
3. **Content gap matrix** — topics × competitors, showing who covers what and quality level
4. **SERP ownership table** — target keywords × who ranks × SERP features present
5. **Prioritized opportunity list** — the 15-20 highest-value SEO/content opportunities for Incorporate123's redesign, ranked by potential impact

Be specific and evidence-based. When you identify a gap or opportunity, explain WHY it matters for the redesign and what type of page/content should fill it.
```

## Research Session 3: Pricing, Packages & Conversion UX

```
I'm conducting a competitive pricing and conversion analysis for a website redesign in the business formation / privacy incorporation industry. I need a deep teardown of how 5 competitors present their packages, pricing, trust signals, and conversion funnels — focusing on what we can learn for our client's redesign.

## Context

My client is Incorporate123 (incorporate123.co), a privacy-focused formation company. Key facts about their current offering:
- Nevada LLC packages: ~$1,300+ (Gold packages with nominee services)
- Wyoming LLC packages: priced as impulse buys (lower price point)
- Offshore formation: Panama, Hong Kong, BVI, St. Kitts, Nevis
- Shelf companies: aged entities from 2000 ($2,000–$12,000)
- Annual renewal: $525 including nominees
- Key differentiator: year-round nominee services with full corporate minute maintenance (NOT "nominee for a day")
- Free offshore nominees included since 2012
- Accepts cryptocurrency (Bitcoin, Ethereum, XRP, Litecoin, Monero)
- Consultant-guided service via phone/email (not automated forms)
- Client prefers "Order Now" over "Call Now" — wants self-service ordering

The redesign will restructure from overlapping packages to base packages with optional add-ons, reduce decision fatigue, and present pricing transparently.

## The 5 Competitors

1. **NCH Inc** — nchinc.com — ~$1,300 base (Nevada). Business credit coaching. No nominees.
2. **Anderson Advisors** — andersonadvisors.com — Attorney-led. Free "strategy sessions" as lead gen. Premium positioning.
3. **ShieldCorp** — shieldcorp.net — Nominee services. Smaller operation.
4. **Wyoming Corporate Services** — wyomingcompany.com — Base LLC $515–$1,140. Nominee $400/yr add-on. Attorney-privilege packages $1,765–$2,700.
5. **ZenBusiness** — zenbusiness.com — $0 formation + state fees. Three tiers ($0 / ~$199 / ~$299). Budget benchmark.

## What I Need Analyzed Per Competitor

### Package & Pricing Architecture
- How many tiers/packages exist and what are they called?
- Exact pricing (or pricing ranges) for each tier
- What's included vs. add-on for each tier
- How they present "state fees" — included or separate?
- Whether pricing is transparent on the site or hidden behind consultation/contact forms
- Annual/recurring costs — what does year 2+ look like?
- Package comparison tables — do they exist? How are they structured?
- "Most Popular" or recommended tier highlighting

### Pricing Page UX & Design
- Page layout and visual hierarchy
- How features/inclusions are listed (checkmarks, feature tables, expandable sections)
- How they differentiate tiers visually
- CTA button copy, color, placement
- Use of pricing psychology: anchoring, decoy pricing, charm pricing ($X99), urgency/scarcity
- Mobile pricing page experience
- Use of progressive disclosure (showing basic info first, details on click/scroll)

### Trust Signals & Social Proof
- Review/rating display (where, how prominent, which platforms cited)
- Trust badges and certifications (BBB, SSL, guarantees)
- Testimonials — placement, format (text, video, case study), specificity
- Years in business / number of businesses formed claims
- Money-back guarantees or satisfaction promises
- Attorney/expert credentials displayed
- Media mentions or press logos

### Conversion Funnel
- Complete user journey from homepage → service selection → package selection → checkout
- Total number of steps/pages in the purchase process
- Whether checkout is self-service or requires human contact
- Upsell/cross-sell strategy during checkout (what gets offered, how aggressive)
- Lead capture for non-purchasers (email capture, free resources, consultation booking)
- Form complexity at each stage (how many fields, required vs. optional)
- Live chat / chatbot presence and role in conversion
- Phone number prominence and accessibility

### Value Proposition Messaging
- How each competitor positions their primary value proposition on the homepage
- Headline copy and subheadline messaging
- How they differentiate from other providers (explicit comparison or implicit positioning)
- Whether they address privacy, asset protection, or both — and how prominently
- How they handle the "why us" or "why choose [company]" messaging
- Whether they use comparison pages against specific competitors

## Additional Analysis Needed

### Pricing Architecture Pattern Recognition
Across all 5 competitors, identify:
- Which use a loss-leader model vs. premium consultative model vs. transparent flat-rate model
- How each handles the "formation fee + state fee" breakdown
- Whether annual renewal pricing is clear or buried
- The total Year 1 cost vs. total Year 2+ cost for equivalent services

### Trust Signal Effectiveness
Research what trust signals are most effective for high-consideration, premium service purchases:
- What does current UX research say about trust badges, testimonials, guarantees for $500+ services?
- How important are reviews for privacy-focused services where clients may be reluctant to leave public reviews?
- What alternative trust-building strategies work when you don't have thousands of reviews?

### Pricing Strategy Recommendations
Based on the competitive landscape, analyze:
- Where does the pricing white space exist between budget ($0–$300) and premium ($1,300+)?
- How should a "base + add-ons" model be structured to compete effectively?
- What pricing transparency level is optimal — full pricing displayed vs. "starting at" vs. consultation-first?
- How should Incorporate123 present their annual renewal ($525 including nominees) as a competitive advantage vs. Wyoming Corporate Services ($545+)?

## Deliverable Format

1. **Per-competitor teardown** with screenshots or detailed descriptions of pricing pages, package presentation, and checkout flow
2. **Pricing comparison matrix** — competitors as columns, features/inclusions as rows, with actual prices
3. **Trust signal inventory** — what each competitor uses, where they place it, and estimated effectiveness
4. **Conversion funnel map** — steps from landing to purchase for each competitor
5. **UX patterns worth adopting** — specific design and presentation patterns that work well
6. **UX patterns to avoid** — what competitors do poorly and we should not replicate
7. **Strategic pricing recommendations** for Incorporate123's package restructuring, grounded in competitive data

Focus on actionable findings — every observation should connect back to "what should Incorporate123 do differently on the redesigned site."
```

## Post-Research Synthesis Prompt

After running all three sessions above, use this prompt to synthesize findings into a unified competitive analysis:

```
I've completed competitive analysis research across three dimensions for Incorporate123's website redesign. I'm going to share the findings from all three research sessions. I need you to synthesize everything into a unified competitive analysis deliverable.

[PASTE FINDINGS FROM ALL 3 SESSIONS]

## Synthesis Tasks

1. **Competitive Positioning Map** — Place all 5 competitors on a 2×2 matrix: X-axis = Privacy/Asset Protection Expertise (low → high), Y-axis = Digital Experience Quality (basic → advanced). Identify the empty quadrant that represents Incorporate123's target positioning.

2. **Per-Competitor Profile** — For each of the 5 competitors, create a concise profile that includes:
   - One-paragraph summary of who they are and how they compete
   - Site structure grade (A–F) with rationale
   - SEO positioning grade (A–F) with rationale
   - Pricing/conversion grade (A–F) with rationale
   - SWOT analysis focused on website/digital presence
   - Top 3 things to learn from them
   - Top 3 weaknesses to exploit

3. **Cross-Competitor Patterns** — Identify:
   - What ALL competitors do (industry table stakes)
   - What ONLY 1-2 competitors do (potential differentiators)
   - What NO competitor does well (blue ocean opportunities)

4. **Top 20 Redesign Recommendations** — Prioritized by impact, organized by:
   - Information Architecture decisions
   - Content Strategy decisions
   - SEO decisions
   - Pricing/Package Presentation decisions
   - Conversion Optimization decisions
   - Trust & Credibility decisions

5. **Spider/Radar Chart Data** — Score each competitor 1-10 on these dimensions:
   - Content Quality & Depth
   - SEO Performance
   - UX & Navigation
   - Pricing Transparency
   - Trust Signals
   - Conversion Optimization
   - Mobile Experience
   - Visual Design / Modernity
   - Privacy/AP Expertise Depth
   - Self-Service Capability

Format the final output as a structured report with clear sections, tables, and actionable recommendations. Every recommendation should reference specific competitive evidence that supports it.
```

## Execution Notes

**Sequence:** Run Session 1 → 2 → 3 → Synthesis. Each session builds context for the next.

**Time estimate:** Each session takes 15-30 minutes for deep research to complete. Total including synthesis: ~2 hours of active prompting + review time.

**What these prompts DON'T replace:** Screaming Frog crawls (for precise URL/page counts), SEMrush/Ahrefs data exports (for exact keyword rankings and backlink metrics), and PageSpeed Insights runs (for Core Web Vitals scores). Use the research output to guide and contextualize the tool-based data, not replace it.

**Tip:** Save each research session's output as a separate document in the project knowledge base. This makes the synthesis prompt more effective and preserves the raw research for future reference.
