# Wyoming vs Nevada for Asset Protection

Both Wyoming and Nevada are considered top-tier states for asset protection, and both are frequently recommended by attorneys who specialize in entity structuring. However, the details matter — particularly for single-member LLCs, fraudulent transfer lookback periods, and ongoing costs. This comparison breaks down the specific differences that affect how well your assets are protected in practice.

## Charging Order Protection: The Core Difference

The charging order is the most important asset protection mechanism for LLC owners. When a creditor obtains a judgment against you personally (not against the LLC), the creditor seeks to reach your LLC interest to satisfy that judgment. In states with strong charging order protection, the creditor's only option is a charging order — a lien on future distributions from the LLC. The creditor cannot seize your membership interest, force a liquidation, or take over management.

**Wyoming** codifies the charging order as the exclusive remedy for creditors of LLC members under W.S. 17-29-503. The statute applies to all LLCs regardless of the number of members. This is significant because it explicitly protects single-member LLCs — the most common structure for individual entrepreneurs.

**Nevada** provides charging order protection under NRS 86.401, and its statute describes the charging order as the exclusive remedy for judgment creditors of multi-member LLCs. For single-member LLCs, the protection is less explicitly established. While most Nevada practitioners believe the protection applies, the statutory language does not address single-member entities as clearly as Wyoming's, and there is limited Nevada case law directly on point.

For business owners operating through a single-member LLC — which describes the majority of our clients — Wyoming's explicit statutory protection removes a layer of uncertainty that exists in Nevada.

## Fraudulent Transfer Lookback Periods

When you transfer assets into an LLC, a creditor may challenge that transfer as fraudulent if it was made with the intent to defraud creditors or if it rendered you insolvent. Each state defines a lookback period during which such transfers can be challenged.

**Wyoming** has adopted a one-year lookback period under its version of the Uniform Voidable Transactions Act. This is the shortest lookback period among leading asset protection states. Assets transferred to a Wyoming LLC more than one year before a claim arises are generally beyond challenge.

**Nevada** uses a two-year lookback period under NRS 112.180. While still reasonable, this gives creditors twice as long to challenge asset transfers compared to Wyoming.

The practical implication: if you are planning an asset protection structure, Wyoming allows you to reach the "safe harbor" one year sooner than Nevada. For business owners who are proactively structuring their assets (rather than reacting to an existing claim), this difference may not be decisive — but it is a clear advantage.

## Director and Officer Liability

This is one area where Nevada has a genuine edge. Nevada's director and officer liability protections (NRS 78.138) are among the strongest in the country. Directors and officers of Nevada corporations are not personally liable for any act or omission as a director or officer unless it is proven that the act or omission constituted a breach of fiduciary duty that involved intentional misconduct, fraud, or a knowing violation of law.

Wyoming provides strong liability protections as well, but Nevada's statute is more explicitly protective and has been tested in more corporate litigation. For businesses structured as corporations (rather than LLCs), Nevada's director liability shield is a meaningful advantage.

For LLCs — where the concept of directors and officers is replaced by managers and members — the practical difference is smaller because LLC liability is primarily governed by the operating agreement and the charging order framework rather than by director liability statutes.

## Privacy and Asset Protection Working Together

Privacy and asset protection are complementary strategies. Privacy prevents potential plaintiffs from discovering what assets you hold and how they are structured. Asset protection provides legal barriers if they do discover and pursue those assets.

Wyoming offers stronger privacy integration because the state does not require public disclosure of officers, managers, or members. Combined with nominee services, a Wyoming LLC is extremely difficult to trace to its beneficial owner through public records alone.

Nevada requires nominee services to achieve equivalent privacy because of the mandatory Initial List of Officers. With nominees in place, the privacy outcome is comparable — but it requires more filings, more management, and more cost to maintain.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**I only have one member in my LLC. Does this matter?**
Yes, significantly. Wyoming explicitly protects single-member LLCs with the exclusive charging order remedy. Nevada's protection for single-member LLCs is less settled. If you are the sole owner of your LLC, Wyoming provides clearer statutory protection.

**What if I add my spouse as a member to make it multi-member?**
This is a common strategy to strengthen charging order protection in states where single-member protections are uncertain. However, it adds complexity to tax filing and operating agreement structure. In Wyoming, this workaround is unnecessary because single-member LLCs already receive full protection.

**Can I have both a Wyoming and a Nevada entity?**
Yes. Some asset protection strategies use multiple entities across jurisdictions. For example, a Wyoming holding LLC that owns a Nevada operating LLC. This is an advanced structure that should be designed with an asset protection attorney.

**Does entity age affect asset protection?**
Not directly, but the timing of asset transfers matters. Assets placed into the LLC well before any claim arises are more defensible than recent transfers. Wyoming's one-year lookback is advantageous here.

**Which is better for real estate — Wyoming or Nevada?**
For holding real estate in either state, form in the state where the property is located to simplify title and recording. For holding out-of-state real estate (e.g., California property), Wyoming provides the stronger home-entity protections.

**Do these protections apply if I'm sued in a different state?**
Generally yes — the asset protection of your LLC is determined by the law of the state of formation. However, courts in your home state may apply their own public policy in extreme cases. Forming in a strong protection state like Wyoming gives you the strongest starting position.

**Should I also carry insurance if I have asset protection?**
Yes. Asset protection through entity structuring is one layer of a comprehensive strategy. Liability insurance, umbrella policies, and professional insurance provide additional protection and are recommended alongside proper entity structuring.

**Can a domestic asset protection trust be used with my LLC?**
Yes. Both Wyoming and Nevada allow domestic asset protection trusts (DAPTs/NAPTs) that can hold LLC membership interests, creating a layered structure. This is an advanced strategy that should be designed with an asset protection attorney.

**How does bankruptcy affect charging order protection?**
In bankruptcy, a trustee may have broader powers than an ordinary judgment creditor. However, Wyoming's strong charging order protections still provide meaningful advantages. The specifics depend on the type of bankruptcy (Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 11) and the circumstances. Consult a bankruptcy attorney for guidance specific to your situation.

**Is a multi-member LLC always better than a single-member LLC for asset protection?**
In Wyoming, single-member and multi-member LLCs receive the same exclusive charging order protection. In Nevada and some other states, multi-member LLCs may receive stronger protection. If you are forming in Wyoming, the number of members does not affect the level of charging order protection.
