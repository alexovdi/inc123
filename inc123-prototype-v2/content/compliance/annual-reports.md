# Annual Reports & Renewal

Every state that allows you to form an LLC or corporation also requires you to file periodic reports confirming your entity's basic information — registered agent, principal office address, officers or managers, and other details. These are generally called annual reports, though the exact name, frequency, and content vary by state.

Missing an annual report deadline is one of the most common compliance failures among business owners. It is also one of the most avoidable. The consequences range from late fees and penalties to administrative dissolution, which means your entity ceases to exist as a legal person and your liability protections evaporate.

### Wyoming

Wyoming's annual report is straightforward and inexpensive. Key details:

- **Filing fee:** $62/year (or $62 minimum, calculated based on assets located in Wyoming)
- **Due date:** First day of the anniversary month of formation
- **Filed with:** Wyoming Secretary of State
- **What it includes:** Confirmation of registered agent, principal office address, and basic entity information
- **Penalty for late filing:** Entity becomes delinquent; after two consecutive years of non-filing, the state administratively dissolves the entity

Wyoming does not require a separate business license at the state level. The annual report is the only required annual filing, making Wyoming one of the simplest and least expensive states for ongoing compliance.

### Nevada

Nevada's annual requirements are more involved and more expensive. There are two separate filings:

**Annual List of Officers/Directors/Managers:**

- **Filing fee:** $150 for LLCs, $150+ for corporations
- **Due date:** Last day of the anniversary month of formation
- **What it includes:** Names and addresses of all officers, directors (corporations), or managers (LLCs)

**State Business License:**

- **Filing fee:** $200 for LLCs, $500 for corporations
- **Due date:** Same as the annual list
- **Penalty for non-renewal:** Fines of $1,000 to $10,000 under NRS 76.180 — _in plain English, this means Nevada can fine you up to $10,000 for operating any business without a current state business license, even if your entity is otherwise in good standing_ — for operating without a valid business license

Both filings are required — missing either one results in penalties and eventual revocation. The initial list is due by the last day of the month following formation, and annual lists are due every year thereafter on the anniversary month.

If you use Incorporate123's nominee/privacy services, we prepare and file the annual list with nominee officers and managers, keeping your name off the public record entirely.

### California

California does not have a traditional annual report for LLCs in the same way other states do. Instead:

- **Statement of Information:** Filed every two years, $20 fee
- **Franchise Tax:** $800/year minimum for all LLCs doing business in California, regardless of revenue
- **Due date for franchise tax:** 15th day of the 4th month after the beginning of the tax year

The $800 annual franchise tax applies to every California LLC — even those that earn no revenue. This is the single most important ongoing cost for California entities and catches many business owners off guard.

### Florida

Florida requires an annual report for all LLCs and corporations:

- **Filing fee:** $138.75
- **Due date:** May 1 of every year (not based on formation date)
- **Late fee:** $400 if filed after May 1
- **What it includes:** Confirmation of registered agent, principal address, and officers/managers

Florida's late fee is steep — $400 on top of the $138.75 filing fee. And unlike most states, every Florida entity has the same deadline regardless of when it was formed, which means there is one date to remember rather than a formation-anniversary date.

## What Happens When You Miss a Deadline

The consequences of missing an annual report are more serious than most business owners realize:

1. **Late fees and penalties** — Every state imposes additional charges for late filings. Nevada can fine you up to $10,000 for operating without a valid business license.
2. **Loss of good standing** — Your entity is flagged as delinquent, which can prevent you from obtaining financing, entering contracts, or filing lawsuits.
3. **Administrative dissolution** — After a specified period of non-compliance, the state will administratively dissolve or revoke your entity. A dissolved entity cannot conduct business.
4. **Personal liability exposure** — If your entity is dissolved or out of compliance and you continue operating, you may be personally liable for business debts incurred during that period.
5. **Reinstatement costs** — Bringing a revoked entity back to good standing requires paying all missed filing fees, penalties, and often additional reinstatement fees. In some states, you must back-file every missed report individually.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between an annual report and an annual list?

Different states use different terminology. Wyoming and Florida call it an "annual report." Nevada calls it an "annual list." The purpose is the same: a required state filing that updates your entity's basic information and confirms it is still active.

### How much do annual report filing fees cost?

Fees vary by state: Wyoming is $62/year, Nevada is $150 (LLC) plus $200 for the business license, California has a $20 biennial statement plus $800 franchise tax, and Florida is $138.75/year.

### When are annual reports due?

It depends on the state. Wyoming: first day of the anniversary month. Nevada: last day of the anniversary month. Florida: May 1 every year. California: every two years for the statement of information.

### What happens if my entity has been dissolved for non-filing?

In most states, you can reinstate the entity by filing all missing reports, paying all back fees and penalties, and filing a reinstatement application. The longer the entity has been dissolved, the more expensive reinstatement becomes. We can handle the entire process.

### Do I need to file an annual report if my business is not active?

Yes. As long as the entity exists with the state, annual filing requirements apply regardless of whether the business is generating revenue. If you do not plan to use the entity, it should be formally dissolved.

### Can Incorporate123 file my annual report in any state?

We directly handle filings in Wyoming, Nevada, California, and Florida. For entities registered in other states, we can arrange filing through our network of state-specific partners.

### What information is included in an annual report?

Typically: the entity's name, registered agent name and address, principal office address, names and addresses of officers/directors/managers, and sometimes a brief description of business activity.

### Will Incorporate123 pay the state filing fees for me?

State filing fees are a pass-through cost billed separately from our service fees. We coordinate the payment so the filing is submitted on time, and you see the exact state fee amount on your invoice.

### What if my entity information has changed since last year?

That is exactly what the annual report is for — it updates the state's records with your current information. We will contact you before each filing to confirm that all details are accurate.

### Can I downgrade from a Gold to a Bronze package?

Yes, but be aware of the implications. Bronze packages do not include virtual office or privacy services. If your entity relied on our Nevada or Wyoming address as its principal office, downgrading means you will need to update your address on file and may need a foreign registration in your home state.

### What is the California franchise tax, and does every LLC pay it?

Yes. California imposes a minimum $800/year franchise tax on every LLC doing business in the state, regardless of revenue. "Doing business" is broadly defined and includes having a member who resides in California. This tax is separate from the biennial Statement of Information filing fee.

### How do I know if my entity is currently in good standing?

You can check with the Secretary of State in your formation state, or we can check for you. When you enroll with Incorporate123, we conduct an initial status review as part of the onboarding process.
