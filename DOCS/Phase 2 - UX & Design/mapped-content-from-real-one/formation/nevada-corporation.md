### No State Income Tax

Nevada imposes no corporate income tax, no personal income tax, and no franchise tax on corporations. A Nevada C-Corporation pays only the federal 21% corporate rate. Shareholders receiving dividends from a Nevada corporation owe no state-level tax on those distributions (though their home state may tax the income). Compare this to California's 8.84% corporate tax rate or Delaware's 8.7% rate.

### Dedicated Business Court

Nevada's business court is a specialized division of the district court system that handles corporate disputes, shareholder litigation, derivative actions, and commercial contract cases. Judges in the business court have deep expertise in Nevada corporate law, which means decisions are more predictable and better informed than in general jurisdiction courts. For corporations that may face shareholder disputes, creditor actions, or contract litigation, this specialization is a meaningful advantage.

### Director and Officer Protections

Nevada Revised Statutes provide some of the broadest director and officer protections in the country:

- **Business judgment rule:** Directors who act in good faith, with the care an ordinarily prudent person would exercise, and in a manner they reasonably believe to be in the best interests of the corporation are not personally liable for their business decisions.
- **Reliance defense:** Directors may rely in good faith on corporate records, reports from officers, legal counsel, accountants, or committees of the board.
- **Indemnification:** Nevada allows corporations to indemnify directors and officers for legal fees, settlements, and judgments arising from their service to the corporation. This can be mandated in the bylaws, providing certainty to individuals serving in these roles.
- **No personal liability for corporate debts:** Shareholders, directors, and officers are not personally liable for the debts or obligations of the corporation, provided the corporate form is maintained.

### Charging Order Protection for Close Corporations

Under NRS 78.746 — _in plain English, this means a creditor who wins a judgment against a shareholder in a Nevada close corporation cannot take the corporation's assets or force it to pay dividends; the creditor can only wait for voluntary distributions_ — a charging order is the exclusive remedy available to a judgment creditor of a stockholder in a Nevada close corporation (more than 1 but fewer than 75 stockholders, not publicly traded, not a professional corporation). This means a creditor who wins a judgment against a shareholder cannot seize corporate assets or force dividend distributions. The creditor receives only an assignee interest and must wait for the corporation to declare distributions voluntarily.

This level of protection for corporate shareholders is unusual among U.S. states and gives Nevada corporations an asset protection feature typically associated only with LLCs.

## Corporate Record-Keeping Requirements

Under NRS 78.105, every Nevada corporation must maintain at its registered office:

- A certified copy of articles of incorporation and all amendments
- A certified copy of bylaws and all amendments
- A stock ledger (or a statement identifying the custodian and location of the stock ledger)

The registered agent must maintain the stock ledger or custodian statement for three years following resignation or dissolution. Stockholders with at least 5% ownership or six months of continuous ownership are entitled to inspect these records.

Incorporate123's Gold package addresses this requirement through our offshore custodian of stock ledger service. Instead of the stock ledger being maintained at the registered agent's office (where it is accessible to third-party subpoenas), a statement identifying the offshore custodian is maintained in its place. This is fully compliant with NRS 78.105(1)(c), which expressly permits this arrangement.

**What is the difference between an LLC and a corporation in Nevada?**
LLCs offer pass-through taxation and flexible management. Corporations issue stock, require a board of directors, and have more formal governance. Most small business owners choose LLCs. Corporations are better for raising investor capital or planning an IPO.

**Do I need to live in Nevada?**
No. Nevada has no residency requirement for shareholders, directors, or officers.

**What is the annual cost to maintain a Nevada corporation?**

**What does the nominee director/officer service include?**
Incorporate123 provides individuals who appear as your corporation's directors and officers on all public filings. You retain complete control through the bylaws, board resolutions, and a confidential agreement.

**Is shareholder information public in Nevada?**
No. Shareholder names never appear on any public filing. Only directors and officers appear on the annual list, and these can be nominees with our Silver and Gold packages.

**Can a Nevada corporation elect S-Corp status?**
Yes. File IRS Form 2553 to elect S-Corp taxation. The corporation must have 100 or fewer shareholders, all of whom are U.S. citizens or residents. The entity remains a Nevada corporation under state law.

**What is the offshore custodian of stock ledger?**
Under NRS 78.105, corporations may keep a statement identifying the custodian of the stock ledger instead of maintaining the ledger itself at the registered office. Our Gold package designates an offshore company secretary as this custodian, placing shareholder information outside the reach of domestic subpoenas.

**How many shares should I authorize?**
Most small corporations authorize 1,000 to 10,000 shares. The number of authorized shares does not affect Nevada filing fees for standard filings.

**Can I convert my LLC to a Nevada corporation?**
Yes. Nevada permits statutory conversions between entity types. Consult a tax professional regarding the tax consequences before converting.

**What is the advantage of Nevada over Delaware for incorporation?**
Nevada has no state income tax (Delaware charges 8.7% corporate tax) and significantly lower annual costs. Nevada also offers charging order protection for close corporation shareholders, which Delaware does not. Delaware's Court of Chancery is primarily advantageous for large publicly traded companies.
