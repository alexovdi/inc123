# Wyoming vs Nevada LLC

Wyoming and Nevada are the two most popular states for out-of-state LLC formation, and for good reason — both offer no state income tax, no franchise tax, strong legal protections, and business-friendly regulatory environments. But they are not interchangeable. The differences in filing requirements, state fees, privacy structures, and specific legal protections make one state significantly better than the other depending on your priorities. This comparison covers everything you need to make an informed decision.

## State Fees and Ongoing Costs

This is where the differences are most immediately tangible. Nevada charges substantially more in state fees than Wyoming, and the gap compounds every year.

**Wyoming** charges a $60 annual report fee for LLCs (based on assets in Wyoming, minimum $60) and $60 for corporations. There is no state business license requirement and no second-tier filings. The total annual state cost for a Wyoming LLC is $60.

**Nevada** requires an annual list of officers ($150), a state business license (LLC $200/year, Corp $500/year), and various filing fees. The total annual state cost for a Nevada LLC is at least $350 — nearly six times Wyoming's cost. For corporations, the gap is even wider due to the $500 business license.

Over a 10-year period, the cumulative state fee difference for an LLC is approximately $2,900 ($600 in Wyoming vs $3,500+ in Nevada). This is state fees alone, before accounting for any service provider charges.

## Privacy

Both states can provide complete ownership privacy when paired with nominee services, but the mechanisms differ.

**Wyoming** does not require an Initial List of Officers or Managers. The only public document is the articles of organization, which lists the entity name, registered agent, and organizer. With nominee services, no personal information enters the public record at any stage.

**Nevada** requires an Initial List of Officers/Managers within 30 days of formation, and this list is publicly searchable. Without nominee services, your name and address are immediately available to anyone searching the Nevada Secretary of State website. With nominee services, privacy is achievable — but it requires actively replacing your information on every filing rather than simply never providing it in the first place.

**Bottom line:** Wyoming provides structural privacy; Nevada provides achievable privacy. The end result with nominees is similar, but Wyoming's approach is cleaner and leaves no window of exposure.

## Asset Protection

Both states offer strong asset protection, but Wyoming has a meaningful edge for individual entrepreneurs.

Wyoming's charging order protection applies to all LLCs, including single-member entities, by explicit statute. Nevada's charging order protection is established for multi-member LLCs but less clearly settled for single-member LLCs. Since most small business owners form single-member LLCs, this distinction matters.

Wyoming also has a shorter fraudulent transfer lookback period (1 year vs. Nevada's 2 years), which means assets transferred to a Wyoming LLC reach the protection safe harbor sooner.

Nevada's advantage is in director and officer liability protections, which are the strongest in the country. If you are forming a corporation with a board of directors, Nevada's liability shield for directors is a genuine differentiator. For LLCs, this advantage is less relevant.

## Formation Speed

**Wyoming** processes electronic filings within 1-2 business days on standard processing. With rush filing, same-day or next-day processing is available.

**Nevada** processes electronic filings within 3-5 business days on standard processing, and the complete package (articles + Initial List + Business License) takes 5-7 business days. Rush processing is available but Nevada's multi-step filing process still takes longer than Wyoming's single filing.

## Legal Environment

Both states have well-established bodies of corporate and LLC case law, but with different strengths.

**Wyoming** pioneered the LLC in 1977 and has nearly five decades of LLC-specific case law. The state's courts have consistently upheld charging order protections and maintained business-friendly interpretations of its LLC statute.

**Nevada** has a longer history of corporate law and a Business Court established in 2000 that handles commercial disputes. Nevada's corporate case law is extensive, making it a strong choice for corporations. However, Nevada's LLC statute is more recent and has less case law specific to LLC disputes.

## Who Should Choose Wyoming

- Business owners forming an LLC (the most common entity type for small businesses)
- Solo entrepreneurs forming single-member LLCs
- Anyone prioritizing privacy as a primary concern
- Cost-conscious business owners who want maximum protection per dollar
- Business owners with no specific Nevada operational needs

## Who Should Choose Nevada

- Business owners physically located in Nevada
- Companies with existing Nevada operations, contracts, or licenses
- Corporations (not LLCs) that benefit from Nevada's director liability protections
- Business owners who need a Nevada address and presence for credibility purposes

## Frequently Asked Questions

**If Wyoming is better, why does anyone still form in Nevada?**
Decades of marketing. Nevada has spent more on promoting itself as a business formation destination than any other state. The advantages it promotes are real but Wyoming matches or exceeds them on most metrics while costing less. Business owners who are already in Nevada have good reason to form there — but out-of-state entrepreneurs generally get a better result in Wyoming.

**Can I form in Wyoming if I live in Nevada?**
Yes. You can form in Wyoming and, if needed, file a foreign registration in Nevada to conduct business there. However, if your primary operations are in Nevada, forming in Nevada directly may be simpler.

**Do I need a physical presence in the state where I form?**
No. Our registered agent service provides the required physical presence. You do not need to live in, visit, or have an office in either Wyoming or Nevada.

**Will my bank care which state I form in?**
No. Banks accept entities from all states. You will need an EIN and articles of organization regardless of the formation state.

**Is one state better for holding real estate?**
If the real estate is located in Wyoming, form in Wyoming. If in Nevada, form in Nevada. For real estate in other states, Wyoming is generally the preferred home entity because of its stronger privacy and charging order protections, with a foreign registration in the state where the property is located.

**Can I change states later?**
Yes. Most states allow domestication — the process of converting an entity from one state to another without dissolving and re-forming. We can help you convert a Nevada entity to Wyoming (or vice versa) if your needs change.

**What about Delaware?**
Delaware is the default for large corporations and VC-backed startups, but it offers weaker asset protection (no single-member LLC charging order protection), higher annual fees ($300 franchise tax), and no meaningful privacy advantage over Wyoming. For small business owners, Wyoming is the better choice.
