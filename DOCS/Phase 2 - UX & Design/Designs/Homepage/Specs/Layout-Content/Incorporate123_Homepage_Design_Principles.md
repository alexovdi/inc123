## How to Use This Document

This document defines the 12 governing principles for the Incorporate123 homepage design. Every layout decision, content block, and interaction pattern on the homepage should be evaluated against these principles before approval.

Each principle includes the rule itself, the strategic rationale connecting it to project strategy, and specific Do/Don't guidance for practical application during wireframing, prototyping, and visual design.

These principles are derived from the approved Phase 1 deliverables: the Positioning Framework, Brand Guide v2, User Journey Mapping, Validated Site Architecture, Phase 2 UX Design Scope, and the SOW. Where a principle conflicts with a design decision, the principle governs unless the underlying strategy document is formally revised.

## PRINCIPLE 1: Intent-First, Not Geography-First

The homepage asks "what do you need?" not "which state?" Privacy leads the hierarchy, followed by Asset Protection, then Formation. Visitors choose their goal — jurisdiction only enters the picture after intent is established. This is the core of the repositioning from "Nevada incorporation company" to "Privacy, Asset Protection & Business Formation Specialists."

**Strategic Rationale:** The entire competitive strategy depends on this shift. Every competitor's homepage leads with geography or entity type. Leading with intent occupies the white-space position and immediately signals a different kind of company.

**DO:**

- Lead hero messaging with privacy and protection outcomes
- Structure pillar navigation around goals: Privacy, Asset Protection, Formation
- Let state selection happen within pillar content, not as the primary entry point

**DON'T:**

- Open with "Nevada LLC" or "Wyoming Corporation" as the lead message
- Use geography-first navigation (United States > Nevada > Services)
- Default to entity-type language before establishing the visitor's goal

---

## PRINCIPLE 2: 80/20 Progressive Disclosure

80% of visitors scan and decide fast — they need the value proposition, clear routing, and a CTA above the fold. 20% want depth and will scroll. The homepage serves both by front-loading the scannable layer and stacking detail below. Nothing above the fold should require reading a paragraph to understand.

**Strategic Rationale:** David confirmed the site is "super content rich and most people won't read it," but emphasized that details matter for trust. Progressive disclosure resolves this tension — concise by default, expandable for researchers.

**DO:**

- Keep hero content to headline + subhead + CTA — no paragraphs
- Make deeper content accessible via scroll, expand, or click-through
- Use visual hierarchy (size, weight) to separate scan layer from detail layer

**DON'T:**

- Force visitors to read dense text before seeing a CTA
- Hide critical information behind interactions that scanners will miss
- Treat the homepage like a pillar page with full educational content

---

## PRINCIPLE 3: Stage-Skipping Is Mandatory

Not every visitor arrives at the top of the funnel. The Informed Buyer persona lands already knowing what they want — their path is 1–2 pages to checkout. The homepage must offer both "learn more" paths downward into pillar content AND direct "ready to buy" shortcuts to packages. Forcing everyone through education kills conversions for this segment.

**Strategic Rationale:** User journey mapping identified five distinct personas with different path lengths (1–7 pages). The homepage is the only page that serves all five simultaneously. Blocking any segment's natural path creates measurable conversion loss.

**DO:**

- Provide pillar navigation cards for researchers alongside direct package links for buyers
- Include a visible "View Packages" or pricing shortcut above the fold
- Let returning clients access Login without navigating through marketing content

**DON'T:**

- Build a single linear funnel that every visitor must follow
- Bury package pricing below three scrolls of educational content
- Assume all homepage visitors need education before conversion

## PRINCIPLE 4: One Primary + One Secondary CTA Per Section

Every homepage section gets exactly one primary CTA (action verb + outcome) and one secondary CTA (alternative path). The consultation safety net ("Talk to us instead") appears as a subtle link near every self-service CTA — not as a competing button. Three competing button styles in the same section create decision paralysis and dilute conversion.

**Strategic Rationale:** The brand guide explicitly prohibits three competing primary-style buttons. Research on conversion optimization consistently shows that reducing choices increases action rates. The dual-CTA pattern serves both self-service and consultation-preference segments without conflict.

**DO:**

- Use one primary action per section
- Use secondary style for the alternative
- Place "Talk to us instead" as a text link, not a third button

**DON'T:**

- Show three buttons in one section
- Give the consultation CTA equal visual weight to the primary action
- Omit the consultation safety net from any conversion-adjacent section

---

## PRINCIPLE 5: Above-Fold CTA Presence

At least one CTA must be visible without scrolling on both desktop and mobile. The post-content repeat rule then places the CTA again after the main content and again before the footer. On mobile, a sticky bottom CTA bar with the primary action + tap-to-call takes priority over in-content CTAs.

**Strategic Rationale:** This is a non-negotiable UX rule from the brand guide, applied across all page types. The homepage carries the highest traffic volume and the widest range of visitor intent — above-fold presence ensures no one arrives and wonders what to do next.

**DO:**

- Ensure the hero section includes a visible, tappable CTA on all viewports
- Repeat primary CTA after pillar navigation block and before footer
- Implement sticky bottom bar on mobile with primary action + phone icon

**DON'T:**

- Let the hero section be purely informational with no action element
- Rely on navigation links as a substitute for explicit CTAs
- Use the same CTA copy at every repetition — vary the context

## PRINCIPLE 6: Gold-First Package Hierarchy

Whenever packages appear on the homepage, Gold is highlighted as "Most Popular" with a visually elevated card and prominent badge. Silver is visible but subordinate. Bronze gets minimal presence. Always show the renewal rate alongside the formation price. This is the all-inclusive transparency promise in visual form.

**Strategic Rationale:** Gold (Privacy) is the hero product and the highest-margin offering. The positioning framework designates it as the recommended standard for anyone seeking privacy. Visual hierarchy on the homepage sets the expectation that carries through to package pages.

**DO:**

- Display Gold first or center with "Most Popular" badge and elevated styling
- Show both formation price and annual renewal ($1,275 + $525/yr for WY)
- Include a brief value-comparison line ("Includes $2,000+ in services")

**DON'T:**

- Give Bronze equal visual weight or position it as the default
- Show formation price without the renewal rate
- Use "starting at" language — always say "all-inclusive"

## PRINCIPLE 7: Trust Compensates for Zero Reviews

With no public platform reviews at launch, the homepage carries the heaviest trust burden. The trust architecture prescribes six immediate signals: 25-year track record, real Reno office, founder visibility, money-back guarantee, crypto payment badges (privacy alignment signal), and prominent phone number. These are structural elements, not decorative — they compensate for the social proof gap until Trustpilot reviews accumulate.

**Strategic Rationale:** NCH has 12,340+ Trustpilot reviews at 5.0. Anderson has attorney credibility. Incorporate123 has neither. Without systematic trust architecture, the premium pricing ($1,275+) becomes a conversion barrier. Every trust signal earns permission to charge more than the $0 formation competitors.

**DO:**

- Include a dedicated trust signals bar with all six signals
- Make phone number clickable in the header and within trust bar
- Design testimonial section to scale from 2–3 quotes to a full carousel

**DON'T:**

- Treat trust signals as optional decoration in the footer only
- Use generic stock photography of people — it contradicts privacy positioning
- Wait for reviews before building trust infrastructure into the homepage

## PRINCIPLE 8: Privacy Is the Lead Message

David's two selling points are privacy and minimum necessary compliance. The hero headline, the first pillar card, and the primary visual weight all signal "this is a privacy company that also forms businesses" — not the other way around. Privacy gets the dominant position in every homepage element where pillars appear.

**Strategic Rationale:** Privacy keywords have 5,500–9,500 monthly searches with minimal competition from budget providers. Formation keywords are dominated by companies with $50M+ ad spend. Leading with privacy targets the uncontested search landscape and matches the premium self-service positioning.

**DO:**

- Make the hero headline about privacy/protection outcomes, not entity formation
- Position the Privacy pillar card first in the navigation block
- Use privacy-related imagery and language as the dominant visual theme

**DON'T:**

- Lead with "Form Your LLC" or "Start Your Business" as the hero headline
- Give Formation equal visual weight to Privacy in the pillar hierarchy
- Bury privacy messaging below the fold behind formation content

## PRINCIPLE 9: Pillar Color-Coding Starts Here

The homepage is where visitors first encounter the visual language that carries through the entire site. Four distinct pillar identities are established here. If the pillar cards on the homepage don't establish this system clearly, every subsequent page loses its wayfinding cue.

**Strategic Rationale:** The 71-page architecture uses pillar-specific visual coding on every page type. The homepage is the only page where all pillars appear together — it's the teaching moment for the system. Visitors who internalize the association here navigate faster across the site.

**DO:**

- Apply pillar identities consistently to card borders, icons, and accent elements
- Use the same mapping in mega menu, pillar cards, and any pillar references
- Keep pillar accents distinct from the primary CTA to avoid confusion

**DON'T:**

- Use pillar accents for non-pillar elements (e.g., for a generic info box)
- Swap or inconsistently apply identities between homepage and inner pages
- Let pillar card styling rely on text labels alone without visual reinforcement

## PRINCIPLE 10: No Fear-Based Selling

The brand voice is authoritative, educational, transparent, reassuring, and precise. The homepage makes visitors feel informed and confident, not scared into buying. Specific numbers do the persuasion work — $1,275 all-inclusive, 25 years of experience, $525/yr renewal, $2,000+ in included services — not urgency language or threat framing.

**Strategic Rationale:** The positioning framework explicitly prohibits fear-based selling. Incorporate123's target audience is research-oriented professionals with $500K+ in assets — they respond to data and specificity, not emotional pressure. Anderson Advisors already owns the consultation-gated urgency position; competing there is a losing strategy.

**DO:**

- Use specific dollar figures and timeframes as primary persuasion tools
- Frame benefits as outcomes: "your name stays off public records"
- Let comparison data speak for itself: "competitors charge $400–$5,000/yr for nominees alone"

**DON'T:**

- Use "you'll get sued if you don't act now" or any threat framing
- Include countdown timers, scarcity signals, or false urgency
- Write copy that makes visitors feel anxious rather than informed

## PRINCIPLE 11: Mobile-First Conversion

The homepage must convert on mobile with the same effectiveness as desktop. This means a sticky bottom CTA bar (primary action + tap-to-call phone icon), a hamburger menu that maintains pillar structure rather than collapsing to an alphabetical list, and Packages and Login visible above the fold in the mobile menu. Everything that works on desktop must work on a phone screen.

**Strategic Rationale:** The majority of initial visits to service businesses come from mobile. The SOW specifies responsive layouts for every template. The mobile experience is not an adaptation of desktop — it's the primary experience for many visitors, and the homepage is the most-visited mobile page.

**DO:**

- Design the sticky bottom bar as a first-class element, not an afterthought
- Structure the mobile menu around pillars with Packages and Login prominent
- Test touch targets, scroll performance, and CTA visibility on real devices

**DON'T:**

- Flatten the mobile menu into an alphabetical list of all pages
- Hide the phone number behind a menu tap — keep it one tap away
- Let package comparison grids break on narrow viewports without a mobile adaptation

---

## PRINCIPLE 12: Premium Positioning, Never Price Competition

Incorporate123 occupies the premium self-service quadrant that no competitor fills. The homepage must never say "affordable" or "competitive pricing." Instead, it communicates included value: year-round nominees (competitors charge $400–$5,000/yr for this alone), offshore records storage, all state fees included. The price is justified, not apologized for.

**Strategic Rationale:** Budget providers ($0 formation) dominate the price-sensitive market with $50M+ in ad spend. Competing on price is mathematically impossible. The white-space opportunity is premium + self-service — the homepage must make visitors understand why $1,275 is less than what competitors charge for a fraction of the included services.

**DO:**

- Frame pricing as "all-inclusive" with explicit value breakdowns
- Include comparative messaging: "Includes services competitors charge $2,000+/yr for"
- Position the 25-year track record and included services as the price justification

**DON'T:**

- Use the words "affordable," "cheap," "budget," or "competitive pricing"
- Apologize for or hedge around the premium price point
- Lead with price before establishing the value of what's included
