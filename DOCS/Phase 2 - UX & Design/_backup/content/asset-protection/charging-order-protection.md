# Charging Order Protection

If you have looked into asset protection, you have probably encountered the term "charging order." It is the legal mechanism that makes LLC-based asset protection work — the reason a creditor who wins a judgment against you personally cannot simply seize the assets inside your LLC. Understanding how charging orders function, which states provide the strongest protections, and how they apply to your situation is essential to building an effective asset protection plan.

At Incorporate123, we have spent 25 years helping clients structure LLCs in Wyoming and Nevada specifically because these two states offer the strongest charging order protections in the United States. Here is what you need to know.

## What Is a Charging Order?

A charging order is a court order obtained by a judgment creditor that places a lien on a debtor's membership interest in an LLC. It entitles the creditor to receive any distributions that the LLC would otherwise pay to the debtor-member.

Here is what a charging order does:

- Gives the creditor a right to intercept distributions made to the debtor-member
- Creates a lien on the debtor's economic interest in the LLC

Here is what a charging order does **not** do:

- Transfer ownership of LLC assets to the creditor
- Give the creditor the right to seize LLC property
- Allow the creditor to vote on LLC management decisions
- Grant access to LLC records, bank accounts, or financial information
- Force the LLC to make distributions
- Allow foreclosure on the membership interest (in Wyoming and Nevada)

The distinction is critical. A charging order is a passive remedy. The creditor waits for distributions that may never come. Meanwhile, the LLC continues operating under the control of its members, holding its assets, and making management decisions without any creditor interference.

This is fundamentally different from how creditors collect against other types of property. A creditor with a judgment can garnish your bank account, levy your brokerage account, or execute against your real estate. But when those assets are held inside an LLC in Wyoming or Nevada, the charging order is the creditor's only option.

## The Key Statutes: Wyoming and Nevada

### Wyoming Statute 17-29-503

Wyoming's charging order statute is widely regarded as the strongest in the country. The statute establishes that a charging order is the **exclusive remedy** by which a judgment creditor can satisfy a judgment from a member's interest in an LLC. But Wyoming goes further than any other state with subsection (g):

> *"This section provides the exclusive remedy by which a person seeking to enforce a judgment against a judgment debtor, including any judgment debtor who may be the sole member, dissociated member or transferee, may, in the capacity of the judgment creditor, satisfy the judgment from the judgment debtor's transferable interest or from the assets of the limited liability company. Other remedies, including foreclosure on the judgment debtor's limited liability interest and a court order for directions, accounts and inquiries that the judgment debtor might have made are not available to the judgment creditor attempting to satisfy a judgment out of the judgment debtor's interest in the limited liability company and may not be ordered by the court."*

Three critical provisions stand out:

1. **Sole member protection.** Wyoming explicitly extends charging order protection to single-member LLCs. Most states either do not address this or have court decisions that weaken protection for sole members.
2. **No foreclosure.** The statute explicitly prohibits foreclosure on the membership interest. In some states, courts have allowed creditors to foreclose on LLC interests, effectively defeating the protection. Wyoming eliminates this possibility.
3. **No judicial orders.** Courts cannot order the LLC to provide directions, accounts, or inquiries on behalf of the creditor. The creditor is limited to waiting for distributions.

### Nevada NRS 86.401

Nevada's charging order statute provides strong protection as well:

> *"On application to a court of competent jurisdiction by a judgment creditor of a member, the court may charge the member's interest with payment of the unsatisfied amount of the judgment with interest. To the extent so charged, the judgment creditor has only the rights of an assignee of the member's interest."*

> *"This section provides the exclusive remedy by which a judgment creditor of a member or an assignee of a member may satisfy a judgment out of the member's interest of the judgment debtor."*

The "exclusive remedy" language is the key. Nevada courts have consistently held that creditors cannot pursue remedies beyond the charging order — no seizure, no forced sale, no management interference.

Nevada's additional advantages include an extensive body of case law supporting entity protections. Cases like *Rowland v. LePire* (99 Nev. 308, 1983) and *Polaris Indus. Corp. v. Kaplan* (103 Nev. 598, 1987) demonstrate that Nevada courts take a restrictive approach to piercing entity protections, requiring clear evidence of fraud before looking beyond the entity structure.

## Why Charging Orders Deter Creditors

The charging order creates a practical problem for creditors that goes beyond simply blocking asset seizure. Several dynamics make the charging order position deeply unattractive:

### The Phantom Income Problem

If a charging order is issued against your membership interest, the creditor becomes an assignee of your economic interest. Depending on the LLC's tax classification, the creditor may be allocated a share of the LLC's taxable income — even though no cash is distributed. The creditor would owe income tax on money they never received.

This scenario, known as "phantom income," creates a perverse incentive. The creditor might actually lose money by holding the charging order. This dynamic often motivates creditors to negotiate a reduced settlement or abandon the charging order entirely.

### No Control, No Information

The creditor with a charging order has no right to inspect LLC records, review bank statements, audit financial activity, or participate in management decisions. They have no way to determine what the LLC owns, what it earns, or when (if ever) it will distribute funds. This information asymmetry further weakens the creditor's position.

### Distribution Control

Because the LLC is under no obligation to make distributions, the member (you) can defer distributions indefinitely. The LLC can retain earnings, reinvest, or simply hold cash. The creditor has no mechanism to compel payment. Over time, the cost of maintaining the charging order exceeds any reasonable expectation of recovery, and most creditors abandon the effort.

## Why the State of Formation Matters

Not all states provide equal charging order protection. Some critical differences:

- **California, Florida, and several other states** allow courts to foreclose on LLC membership interests or order the LLC to be dissolved, effectively allowing the creditor to access LLC assets.
- **Many states** do not address single-member LLCs, leaving the question to judicial interpretation. Some courts have ruled that charging order protection does not apply when the LLC has only one member.
- **Wyoming** explicitly prohibits foreclosure, dissolution, and other remedies — even for single-member LLCs. The protection is statutory, not dependent on judicial interpretation.
- **Nevada** provides exclusive remedy language and is backed by decades of protective case law, though it does not address single-member LLCs as explicitly as Wyoming.

This is why forming your LLC in Wyoming or Nevada — rather than your home state — is a critical decision. A California LLC provides meaningfully weaker protection than a Wyoming LLC, even if both are structured identically.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the difference between a charging order and a lien on my house?**
A lien on your house can result in a forced sale — the creditor can foreclose and collect from the proceeds. A charging order cannot result in a forced sale of LLC assets (in Wyoming and Nevada). The creditor can only intercept distributions, and the LLC is under no obligation to make them.

**Can a creditor force my LLC to distribute money?**
No. Under both Wyoming Statute 17-29-503 and Nevada NRS 86.401, the LLC cannot be compelled to make distributions. The creditor must wait for voluntary distributions, which may never occur.

**Does charging order protection apply if I am the only member of the LLC?**
In Wyoming, yes — explicitly. Statute 17-29-503(g) specifically includes "any judgment debtor who may be the sole member." In Nevada, the statute provides exclusive remedy language but does not address single-member LLCs as directly. For sole-member entities, we typically recommend Wyoming.

**What is phantom income and how does it affect the creditor?**
When a creditor holds a charging order, they may be allocated taxable income from the LLC even though no cash is distributed. The creditor owes tax on income they never received. This creates financial pressure to settle or abandon the charging order.

**Can a court order my LLC dissolved to pay a creditor?**
In Wyoming, no. The statute explicitly prohibits courts from ordering remedies beyond the charging order. In some other states (including California), courts have ordered dissolution in certain circumstances. This is a major reason to form your LLC in Wyoming rather than your home state.

**Does the charging order expire?**
Charging orders are generally governed by the same rules as the underlying judgment. Judgments typically last 5-20 years depending on the state and can sometimes be renewed. However, if the creditor receives no distributions during this period, the charging order produces no recovery.

**What happens if I have a charging order against me and want to sell my membership interest?**
The charging order creates a lien on your membership interest. Proceeds from a sale of that interest would be subject to the charging order. However, the operating agreement can restrict transfers, and the LLC can redeem the interest under terms that manage the creditor's claim.

**Is a charging order the same as a garnishment?**
No. A garnishment seizes funds directly from a bank account or paycheck. A charging order does not seize anything — it only redirects future voluntary distributions. The LLC retains full control of its assets.

**Does charging order protection work against the IRS?**
Federal tax liens operate under federal law, not state LLC statutes. The IRS has broader collection powers than private creditors and may be able to reach LLC assets in certain circumstances. Charging order protection is designed for private creditor claims, not federal tax obligations.

**Do I need both an LLC and charging order protection?**
They are the same thing. Charging order protection is a feature of LLC law — it comes with the LLC automatically. The question is whether your state's charging order statute is strong enough. Wyoming and Nevada offer the strongest versions; other states may offer weaker protection or none at all for single-member entities.
