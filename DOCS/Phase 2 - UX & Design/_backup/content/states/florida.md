# Florida Business Formation

Florida's combination of no personal income tax, a growing economy, and a business-friendly reputation makes it one of the most popular states for entrepreneurs and business relocations. However, Florida's native privacy protections are moderate at best, and its asset protection statutes are not as strong as Wyoming's or Nevada's. For Florida-based business owners who prioritize privacy and asset protection, the most effective strategy is to form in Wyoming or Nevada and register as a foreign entity in Florida.

## Why Form Outside Florida?

While Florida is a good state to live and do business in, it has limitations when it comes to privacy and asset protection:

- **Moderate Privacy** — Florida requires disclosure of officer, director, and registered agent information in its Annual Report, which is a public record searchable through the Division of Corporations website.
- **Weaker Charging Order Protection** — Florida provides charging order protection for multi-member LLCs, but the protections for single-member LLCs are less established and have been the subject of unfavorable court decisions.
- **Broad Public Records Law** — Florida's Sunshine Law makes most government records, including corporate filings, readily accessible to the public. This extends to business entity records held by the Division of Corporations.
- **No Personal Income Tax** — This is an advantage of Florida itself, not its corporate framework. You retain this benefit regardless of where your entity is formed.

## The Florida Private Strategy

Our recommended approach for Florida-based business owners:

1. **Form in Wyoming or Nevada** — Establish your LLC or corporation in a state with the strongest privacy protections, lowest costs, and most favorable asset protection statutes.
2. **Register as a foreign entity in Florida** — File the appropriate registration with the Florida Division of Corporations. This authorizes your entity to conduct business in Florida.
3. **Operate locally with out-of-state protections** — Your entity is governed by the laws of the formation state (Wyoming or Nevada), including privacy and charging order protections. Florida treats the entity as a registered foreign company.

This is the same approach used by thousands of businesses across Florida and is a standard corporate structuring practice throughout the United States.

## Quick Facts

- **Florida Annual Report Fee:** $138.75 (LLC or Corporation)
- **Florida Personal Income Tax:** None
- **Florida Corporate Income Tax:** 5.5% on net income over $50,000
- **Foreign Entity Registration:** Required if doing business in Florida
- **Annual Report:** Required annually, due by May 1
- **Privacy Level (native FL entity):** Moderate — officer names in public record, searchable online
- **Privacy Level (WY/NV + FL foreign reg):** Strong — formation state records remain private

## Privacy & Nominee Services for Florida Businesses

When you form in Wyoming or Nevada with nominee services and register the entity in Florida, the Florida foreign registration filing shows the entity name and Florida registered agent. The officers, directors, and managers listed in the formation state's records are your nominees, not your personal information.

Florida's broad public records access makes this out-of-state formation strategy particularly valuable. Without it, your name, address, and role are easily searchable by anyone with internet access through the Florida Division of Corporations database.

## Asset Protection for Florida Residents

Florida's asset protection landscape has specific characteristics that business owners should understand:

- **Homestead Exemption** — Florida offers one of the strongest homestead exemptions in the country, protecting your primary residence from most creditors. This is a powerful protection that exists independent of your business structure.
- **Single-Member LLC Vulnerability** — Florida courts have allowed creditors to reach the assets of single-member LLCs through reverse veil-piercing and other theories. Wyoming's exclusive charging order remedy provides significantly stronger protection for single-member entities.
- **Multi-Member LLCs** — Florida provides reasonable charging order protection for multi-member LLCs, though the statutes are not as explicit as Wyoming's or Nevada's.

For comprehensive asset protection, we recommend combining a Wyoming or Nevada formation with Florida's homestead exemption for your residence. Clients with substantial liquid assets may also benefit from [offshore structuring](/offshore/) to add international jurisdictional barriers.

## Compliance

Operating a foreign entity in Florida requires:

- Annual Report filing with the Florida Division of Corporations by May 1 ($138.75)
- Florida corporate income tax returns if the entity has Florida-sourced income exceeding $50,000
- Compliance with the formation state's requirements (annual reports, registered agent, etc.)

Florida does not impose a franchise tax or minimum tax on foreign entities (unlike California's $800 minimum). This makes the dual-state structure more cost-effective for Florida-based businesses.

## FAQ

**Is Florida's lack of income tax a reason to form there?**
Florida's lack of personal income tax benefits you as a resident regardless of where your business entity is formed. Forming in Wyoming or Nevada does not change your Florida income tax situation — you still pay no personal state income tax. The reason to form outside Florida is privacy and asset protection, not tax savings.

**Does the Florida Annual Report expose my information?**
For native Florida entities, yes — the Annual Report includes officer and director names and is publicly searchable online. For foreign entities registered in Florida, the filing shows the entity name and registered agent, with the formation state's records (where your nominees appear) remaining under that state's disclosure rules.

**Is Florida's homestead exemption affected by my business structure?**
No. The homestead exemption protects your primary residence independently of your business entities. In fact, pairing a strong homestead exemption with a Wyoming or Nevada LLC creates a complementary protection strategy: your home is protected by Florida law, and your business assets are protected by the formation state's law.

**Do I need a Florida registered agent?**
Yes. Any foreign entity registered in Florida must designate a Florida registered agent. Our Florida Private Package includes this.

**How much does it cost annually to maintain the dual-state structure?**
You will pay the formation state's annual fees (Wyoming: $62 minimum; Nevada: $350+), our registered agent and service fees, and Florida's $138.75 annual report fee. The total is typically less than you might expect, and the privacy and protection benefits are substantial.
