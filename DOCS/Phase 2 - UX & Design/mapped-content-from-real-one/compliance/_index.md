# Compliance Services

You formed your LLC or corporation for a reason: to protect your personal assets, gain tax advantages, and build a credible business entity. But formation is only the first step. Without ongoing compliance, every one of those benefits is at risk.

States require specific filings, reports, and record-keeping from every active business entity. Miss a deadline, and you face late fees, administrative dissolution, or — worst of all — a court deciding your entity protections no longer apply. This is known as "piercing the corporate veil," and it happens more often than most business owners realize.

## What Business Compliance Actually Means

Business compliance refers to the ongoing legal requirements your LLC or corporation must satisfy to remain in good standing with the state where it was formed — and any additional states where it operates.

These requirements vary by state and entity type, but they generally fall into several categories:

- **Registered agent service** — Every LLC and corporation must maintain a registered agent with a physical address in the state of formation. This agent receives legal documents and official state correspondence on your behalf.
- **Annual reports and renewals** — Most states require an annual or biennial report updating your entity's basic information, along with associated filing fees.
- **Corporate records maintenance** — Bylaws, operating agreements, meeting minutes, stock ledgers, and membership records must be properly prepared and maintained.
- **State business licenses** — Some states, including Nevada, require a separate business license in addition to your entity registration.
- **Multi-state filings** — If your entity operates in states beyond where it was formed, you need foreign registrations in those states, each with their own compliance requirements.
- **Tax obligations** — Post-formation tax filings, franchise taxes, and annual fees vary significantly by state and entity type.

Failing to meet any of these requirements does not just result in fees. It can lead to your entity being revoked, your personal liability protections being stripped, and the IRS disallowing important tax elections.

## Why Compliance Matters More Than Most Owners Think

Many business owners treat compliance as paperwork — something to handle when they get around to it. That approach creates real risk.

### Piercing the Corporate Veil

When a court pierces the corporate veil, it disregards your entity's separate legal existence and holds you personally responsible for business debts and liabilities. Courts look at several factors when deciding whether to pierce the veil:

- Whether the entity maintained proper corporate records and minutes
- Whether stock was actually issued and documented
- Whether the entity was adequately capitalized
- Whether the entity followed its own bylaws or operating agreement
- Whether the entity filed required state reports on time

Every compliance failure gives a plaintiff's attorney another argument that your entity was a "sham" that should be disregarded. Proper compliance removes those arguments entirely.

### Administrative Dissolution

If you miss annual report deadlines or fail to maintain a registered agent, the state can administratively dissolve or revoke your entity. A revoked entity cannot conduct business, enter contracts, or maintain lawsuits. Reinstating a revoked entity involves additional fees, penalty payments, and in some cases, back-filing every missed report.

### IRS Consequences

The IRS requires that certain corporate formalities be observed for tax elections to remain valid. If your S-Corp election is challenged because you never held the required annual meetings or maintained proper records, the tax consequences can be significant — retroactive reclassification, back taxes, penalties, and interest.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What happens if I miss an annual report deadline?

Most states impose late fees and may begin the process of administratively dissolving your entity. In Nevada, failure to file the annual list results in a $150 penalty on top of the filing fee. In Wyoming, your entity may be dissolved after two years of non-filing. The longer you wait, the more expensive reinstatement becomes.

### Do I really need to issue stock or membership interests?

Yes. Failing to issue stock in a corporation or document membership interests in an LLC is one of the most common reasons courts pierce the corporate veil. It signals that you are not treating the entity as a separate legal person, which is exactly what a plaintiff's attorney will argue.

### What is a registered agent, and why is it required?

A registered agent is a person or company authorized to receive legal documents — including lawsuits, subpoenas, and official state notices — on behalf of your entity. Every state requires one, and the agent must have a physical address in the state of formation. You cannot use a P.O. box.

### Can I be my own registered agent?

Technically yes, if you have a physical address in the state of formation and are available during all business hours. For most out-of-state entity owners, that is not practical. Using a professional registered agent also keeps your personal address off public records.

### What is the difference between foreign registration and domestication?

Foreign registration allows your entity to operate in a new state while remaining formed in its home state. You maintain two registrations. Domestication actually moves your entity from one state to another — the original state registration is terminated and the entity continues under the new state's laws.

### What does an offshore secretary do?

An offshore secretary is appointed as a corporate officer and custodian of your stock ledger or membership records. Because the secretary is located outside U.S. jurisdiction, they are not subject to domestic subpoena power. If the Secretary of State requests records from your registered agent, the agent simply provides the name and address of your offshore custodian.

### Do I need compliance services if my business is not active?

Yes. As long as your entity exists with the state, it must meet its filing obligations. An inactive entity that misses deadlines will still be subject to penalties and eventually administrative dissolution. If you no longer need the entity, it should be formally dissolved.
