# Investment Holding LLC

If your assets are held in your personal name — bank accounts, brokerage portfolios, rental properties, intellectual property — they are fully exposed to anyone who obtains a judgment against you. A plaintiff's attorney can locate these assets through a simple public records search tied to your name and Social Security number, and pursue them through standard collection procedures.

An investment holding LLC changes this equation entirely. By transferring your assets into a dedicated LLC that exists solely to hold and manage those assets, you create a legal separation between your personal identity and your wealth. Combined with the [charging order protection](/asset-protection/charging-order-protection/) statutes in Wyoming and Nevada, this structure makes it extraordinarily difficult for a creditor to reach the assets inside your LLC.

## What Is an Investment Holding LLC?

An investment holding LLC is a limited liability company formed for a single purpose: to hold and manage assets. It does not operate a business, sign commercial contracts, employ staff, or engage in activities that could generate liability. It simply owns assets — and in doing so, it removes those assets from your personal name and places them under the protection of state LLC statutes.

Unlike 14 states (including California, New York, Texas, and Virginia) that require an LLC to have an active business purpose, both Wyoming and Nevada allow LLCs to be formed specifically as investment holding companies. A Wyoming or Nevada LLC can legally exist to "hold and manage" assets — nothing more.

Assets eligible for transfer into an investment holding LLC include:

- Cash and bank accounts
- Investment securities (stocks, bonds, mutual funds)
- Notes and receivables
- Investment real estate (rental properties, commercial buildings, vacant land)
- Personal residences (in many cases)
- Patents, trademarks, and copyrights
- Shares in closely held corporations
- Intellectual property and royalty streams

In exchange for transferring assets to the LLC, you receive a membership interest. You maintain control of the assets through the LLC's operating agreement, but the legal ownership belongs to the entity.

## How It Works as Asset Protection

The protection comes from the interaction between LLC ownership and charging order statutes.

When a creditor obtains a personal judgment against you, they conduct an asset search. Because your assets are held by the LLC — not by you personally — the search reveals little tied to your name. Your primary asset is now a membership interest in a privately held LLC, and that membership interest is protected by [charging order statutes](/asset-protection/charging-order-protection/).

Under Wyoming Statute 17-29-503 and Nevada's NRS 86.401, the creditor's exclusive remedy is a charging order: a lien on distributions the LLC makes to you. The creditor cannot force distributions, seize LLC assets, vote on management matters, or foreclose on your interest. Most collection efforts end at this point, because the creditor recognizes that pursuing the claim further will be expensive, time-consuming, and likely fruitless.

This is why the holding LLC must remain a passive entity. If the LLC signs contracts, employs people, or conducts active business, it could generate its own liability — and a creditor who sues the LLC directly would have access to everything it owns. The holding LLC should do nothing that could expose it to a claim.

### The Separation Principle

A properly structured asset protection plan separates assets by risk profile:

- **Safe assets** (cash, investments, real estate equity) go into the holding LLC
- **High-risk activities** (operating businesses, rental management, professional services) are conducted through separate entities

This way, if the operating business faces a lawsuit, the holding LLC's assets are insulated. And if you personally face a claim, charging order protection prevents the creditor from reaching into the holding LLC.

For clients with significant real estate holdings, we often recommend a multi-entity approach: one holding LLC that owns membership interests in individual property LLCs. This creates both vertical protection (from personal creditors) and horizontal protection (isolating properties from each other). ## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the difference between a holding LLC and an operating LLC?**
A holding LLC exists solely to own and manage assets. It does not conduct business, sign contracts, or engage in activities that could create liability. An operating LLC runs an active business — it signs leases, employs staff, and serves customers. Keeping these functions separate is essential: if the operating LLC faces a lawsuit, the holding LLC's assets are insulated.

**Can a holding LLC own real estate?**
Yes. Real estate is one of the most common assets held in investment holding LLCs. The property deed is transferred from your personal name to the LLC. Rental income flows into the LLC, and the property equity is protected by charging order statutes.

**Do I need a separate bank account for the LLC?**
Yes. Maintaining a separate bank account is essential. Commingling personal funds with LLC funds can be used as evidence to pierce the corporate veil and defeat the LLC's protective structure. We assist with corporate bank account setup as part of our formation package.

**Can I put my personal residence in a holding LLC?**
In many cases, yes. However, there are considerations related to homestead exemptions, mortgage due-on-sale clauses, and property tax reassessment that vary by state. We discuss these factors during your consultation and advise on whether this transfer is appropriate for your situation.

**What happens to my investments inside the LLC?**
Your investments continue to function as they did before — they simply belong to the LLC rather than to you personally. You can buy and sell securities, collect dividends, and manage the portfolio through the LLC's brokerage account. The practical difference is minimal; the legal protection is substantial.

**How many assets should I put in one LLC?**
This depends on the type and value of your assets. For liquid assets (cash, securities), a single holding LLC is often sufficient. For real estate, we frequently recommend separate LLCs for each property or small groups of properties. We advise on the optimal structure during your consultation.

**Does the LLC need to file a tax return?**
A single-member LLC is treated as a disregarded entity by the IRS by default, meaning it does not file a separate tax return — its income is reported on your personal return. Multi-member LLCs file Form 1065 as a partnership. We recommend consulting with your CPA on the optimal tax treatment.

**What if the LLC itself gets sued?**
If the LLC generates liability — through a contract dispute, a slip-and-fall on LLC-owned property, or any other claim — the assets inside that LLC are at risk. This is why the holding LLC must not conduct active business. Active business operations should be handled by a separate operating entity.

**Can I have multiple holding LLCs?**
Yes. Many clients with diverse asset types use multiple LLCs — one for securities, another for real estate, another for intellectual property. This provides additional isolation between asset categories.
