# Nevada Privacy

Nevada has been the go-to formation state for business privacy since the 1990s, and for good reason. Its statutes explicitly support nominee structures, its dedicated business court provides predictable legal outcomes, and its tax environment eliminates state-level filing exposure. While Wyoming has emerged as a strong competitor on cost and statutory privacy, Nevada remains the preferred jurisdiction for business owners who value its established legal infrastructure and deep body of case law.

## Nevada's Privacy Framework

Nevada's privacy protections for business entities are codified across several statutes, each addressing a specific vulnerability in the public record.

**Nominee-friendly statutes.** Nevada's Revised Statutes explicitly contemplate and support the use of nominees in business entities. NRS 78.105 (for corporations) and NRS 86.241 (for LLCs) establish the recordkeeping framework that makes nominee structures legally clear and operationally practical. Unlike states where nominee arrangements exist in a legal gray area, Nevada's code directly addresses how entities with nominees maintain compliance.

**No public member/shareholder disclosure.** Nevada does not require LLC member names in articles of organization. For corporations, shareholder names are not part of the public filing. Officers and directors are listed in the annual filing, which is why nominee services are critical — our nominees fill those positions so your name never appears.

**Dedicated business court.** Nevada's Business Court, established within the Clark County and Washoe County district courts, handles complex commercial disputes with judges who specialize in entity law. This matters for privacy because legal challenges to entity structures — including nominee arrangements — are adjudicated by judges with deep expertise in Nevada business law, producing more predictable outcomes.

**No state income tax.** Nevada has no personal income tax, no corporate income tax, and no franchise tax on general business entities. Beyond the obvious financial benefit, this eliminates an entire category of state filings that could potentially expose ownership information or trigger information-sharing with other state agencies.

## The Record Keeper Summons Problem

Government agencies — including the IRS — increasingly use third-party record keeper summonses to obtain information directly from registered agents, sometimes without notifying the entity itself. The registered agent is a convenient target: every company must have one, they are listed in the public record, and they must be available to receive service of process.

A typical summons demands "copies of all contact information, correspondence, documents retained for or sent to [the entity], all legal documents, lists of officers, names, telephone numbers and addresses of all associated parties, and any other documents in your possession without exclusion or limitation."

Our offshore custodian structure directly addresses this vulnerability. Because the membership listing and stock ledger are maintained by an offshore secretary — not by the domestic registered agent — the registered agent's response to such a summons does not include ownership information. The agent provides the custodian's name and offshore address, and the requesting agency must then attempt to obtain records from a jurisdiction outside U.S. court authority.

**Is Nevada or Wyoming better for privacy?**
Both offer strong privacy protections. Wyoming has a statutory advantage (no member disclosure requirement in the LLC Act) and lower costs. Nevada has a deeper body of case law, explicitly nominee-friendly statutes, and a specialized business court. We recommend Wyoming for most privacy-focused formations, but Nevada is the better choice for clients who value its legal infrastructure or have Nevada-specific business reasons.

**Does Nevada require any public disclosure of LLC members?**
No. Nevada does not require member names in articles of organization. For manager-managed LLCs, the manager's name appears in filings — which is why nominee manager service is essential for privacy.

**What is NRS 86.241 and why does it matter?**
NRS 86.241 establishes recordkeeping requirements for Nevada LLCs, including the obligation to maintain an operating agreement and membership records at the registered office. This statute is why the offshore custodian structure is so important — it ensures that the records the registered agent maintains do not contain personal ownership details.

**What is the dedicated business court?**
Nevada's Business Court is a division within the district court system (Clark County and Washoe County) where judges specialize in commercial and entity law disputes.
