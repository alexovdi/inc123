# Wyoming vs Nevada for Privacy

If privacy is your primary reason for choosing a formation state, the decision between Wyoming and Nevada comes down to structural differences in what each state requires you to disclose publicly, how much that privacy costs to maintain, and what additional protections each state provides. Both states can deliver complete ownership privacy when paired with nominee services — but the path to get there and the ongoing cost differ significantly.

## Wyoming Privacy: Structural Advantage

Wyoming's privacy advantage is built into the state's filing structure. When you form an LLC in Wyoming, the Secretary of State does not require an Initial List of Officers, Managers, or Members. The only document filed publicly is the articles of organization, which lists the entity name, registered agent, and organizer. If a nominee service is used as the organizer, your name never touches the public record at any point during the formation process.

Wyoming's annual report requires disclosure of the entity's principal office address but does not require listing individual officers or members. With nominee services in place, the annual report is filed under nominee names, and the principal office address is our Wyoming address — not yours.

This means Wyoming provides a baseline level of privacy even without nominee services. Your name does not appear on the articles or the annual report unless you choose to list yourself. Nominee services add an additional layer by ensuring that the organizer listed on the articles is also a nominee, creating complete separation between you and the public record.

The practical result: anyone searching the Wyoming Secretary of State database for your entity will find the entity name, our registered agent address, and nominee names. They will not find your name, your home address, or any information linking the entity to you personally.

## Nevada Privacy: Achievable but Requires More Work

Nevada's privacy situation is more complicated. The state requires an Initial List of Officers and Managers to be filed with the Secretary of State within 30 days of formation. This list includes the names and addresses of all officers, directors (Corporation), or managers/managing members (LLC). This filing is publicly searchable on the Nevada Secretary of State website.

Without nominee services, forming a Nevada LLC or Corporation means your name and address will appear on a publicly searchable database. This is the critical difference from Wyoming: Nevada collects and publishes this information by default. Privacy in Nevada is only achievable through nominee services — it is not a feature of the state's filing structure itself.

With nominee services in place, Nevada can provide privacy comparable to Wyoming's. Our nominees appear on the Initial List and on every subsequent annual filing. However, the process is more involved: we must file the Initial List within 30 days, manage the annual list renewal (which is more expensive than Wyoming's annual report), and maintain the State Business License — all with nominee names on every document.

Nevada also requires a State Business License, which adds another public filing. While the business license itself does not typically reveal beneficial ownership when nominees are used, it represents one more document to manage and one more annual fee to pay.

## When Nevada Makes Sense Despite Higher Cost

Nevada is the right choice for privacy when you have a specific reason to be incorporated in Nevada:

- **Physical operations in Nevada** — If your business operates from a Nevada location, having a Nevada entity simplifies compliance and avoids the need for foreign registration.
- **Nevada-specific contracts or licenses** — Some contracts, professional licenses, or government programs require a Nevada entity.
- **Established Nevada presence** — If you already have office space, employees, or business relationships in Nevada, forming locally may be more practical.
- **Preference for Nevada courts** — Nevada's corporate case law is extensive, and some business owners prefer having disputes resolved under Nevada law.

If none of these apply, Wyoming provides equivalent or better privacy at lower cost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**If I choose Wyoming, can I still operate in Nevada?**
Yes. You can file a foreign registration in Nevada ($149 add-on) to legally conduct business there. Your Wyoming entity remains the home entity, and the foreign registration allows operations in Nevada.

**Is Wyoming privacy really better than Nevada's?**
The privacy outcome is the same when both include nominee services — your name stays off the public record. The difference is that Wyoming achieves this structurally (the state does not collect the information), while Nevada requires nominee services to prevent your name from appearing on mandatory public filings.

**Can someone still find out I own a Wyoming LLC?**
Not through public records. A court order or subpoena directed at the LLC could compel disclosure of membership information, but this requires active litigation — not a casual database search.

**Why do so many people still choose Nevada?**
Marketing. Nevada has been aggressively promoted as a privacy and business-friendly state for decades. The state's advantages are real, but Wyoming has quietly matched or exceeded Nevada on nearly every metric while maintaining lower costs.

**Does the state I choose affect my federal taxes?**
No. Federal tax treatment is determined by the entity type and your tax election (sole proprietorship, partnership, S-Corp, or C-Corp), not by the state of formation. Both Wyoming and Nevada have no state income tax.

**What if I already have a Nevada entity and want to switch to Wyoming?**
You can form a new Wyoming entity and transfer assets, or in some cases domesticate (convert) your Nevada entity to Wyoming. Contact us to discuss the most efficient approach for your situation.

**Are there any situations where Nevada privacy is actually better than Wyoming's?**
With nominee services in place, the privacy outcome is effectively identical. Nevada's Business Court may be preferable for certain commercial disputes, but this is a litigation preference, not a privacy advantage. For privacy specifically, Wyoming is the stronger structural choice.

**Does the Beneficial Ownership Information (BOI) reporting affect privacy?**
The Corporate Transparency Act requires beneficial ownership reporting to FinCEN regardless of state of formation. However, the BOI database is not publicly accessible — it is available only to law enforcement and certain financial institutions under specific conditions. State-level privacy (through nominees) remains the primary protection against public record searches.

**Can I use nominee services for an existing entity?**
Yes, but adding nominees to an existing entity requires amending state filings to replace your name with nominee names. In Nevada, your name will have already appeared on historical filings that cannot be retroactively changed. Starting with nominees at formation avoids this issue entirely.

**What about domain registration privacy?**
Entity privacy and domain privacy are separate. If you register a website domain using your entity, use WHOIS privacy or a privacy-forward registrar. Linking your entity name to a domain without privacy protection can undermine the privacy you established through formation.
