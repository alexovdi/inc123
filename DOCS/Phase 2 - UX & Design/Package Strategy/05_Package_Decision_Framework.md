Start here. Your answer determines which branch of packages to explore.

**You are forming a business and your top priority is keeping your personal name off public records.** You may be a public figure, a professional with liability exposure, or someone who simply values personal privacy in business dealings.

<summary>Frequently Asked Questions: Privacy</summary>

**Q: What does "nominee" mean?**
A nominee is a person whose name appears on your company's public filings (such as the list of officers or directors filed with the state) instead of yours. Incorporate123's team serves as your nominees year-round, so your personal name never appears in state databases. You remain the true owner and retain full control.

**Q: Can someone still find out I own the company?**
With a Gold package, your name does not appear on any state-filed documents. The operating agreement (which names you as the true owner) is a private document — it is not filed with any government agency. Combined with offshore records storage, your ownership information stays out of public and domestic databases.

**Q: Do I need a Gold package to get nominee services, or can I add them to Silver?**
Nominee services are only available as part of the Gold tier. They require year-round management and cannot be purchased as a standalone add-on.

### B. "I want to protect my personal assets from business liabilities."

**You own assets — real estate, investments, savings — and you want a legal barrier between your business activities and your personal wealth.** You may already operate a business or you may be forming one with liability risk (consulting, real estate, contracting).

**Your path:** Gold packages, with a strong lean toward Nevada Gold. Nevada's charging order protection statutes are among the strongest in the country, making it harder for a creditor to reach your personal assets through your LLC.

<details>
<summary>Frequently Asked Questions: Asset Protection</summary>

**Q: How does an LLC protect my personal assets?**
An LLC creates a legal separation between you and your business. If your business is sued, creditors can typically only go after business assets — not your home, savings, or personal property. This protection requires maintaining your LLC properly (annual filings, separate bank accounts, proper records), which is why our Gold and Silver packages include compliance management.

**Q: Why is Nevada better than Wyoming for asset protection?**
Both states offer strong protection. Nevada's advantage is its charging order protection statute, which limits creditors to a "charging order" against LLC distributions — they cannot force the LLC to make distributions or seize LLC assets. Nevada also has no state income tax and no information-sharing agreements with the IRS.

**Q: Do I need Gold for asset protection, or is Silver enough?**
Silver gives you the LLC itself (which provides the legal barrier) plus compliance management. Gold adds nominee privacy on top, so creditors cannot even easily identify you as the owner. If your concern is purely structural protection, Silver works. If you also want privacy from litigation discovery, Gold is stronger.

<summary>Frequently Asked Questions: Shelf Companies</summary>

**Q: What is a shelf company?**
A shelf company is a legal entity (LLC or Corporation) that was formed in the past and has been maintained in good standing but has never conducted any business, opened bank accounts, or entered contracts. It has a clean history and an established formation date.

**Q: Why would I want an older entity?**
Age can matter in several situations: applying for business credit lines (lenders may look at how long the entity has existed), bidding on government or corporate contracts (some require a minimum business history), and establishing credibility with vendors or partners who prefer working with established companies.

**Q: Are shelf companies legal?**
Yes. Purchasing and using a shelf company is completely legal. What matters is how you represent the company after purchase — you cannot claim the entity has been actively doing business for years if it has not.

## Step 3: LLC or Corporation?

Regardless of which package you chose above, you need to pick an entity type. Here is the decision in plain terms.

### Choose LLC if:

- You want **flexible management** — no board of directors, officers, or formal meeting requirements
- You prefer **pass-through taxation** — profits and losses flow to your personal tax return (no double taxation)
- You are forming with **one or a few members** and want simple ownership structure
- You want an **operating agreement** (private document) rather than bylaws and corporate minutes

### Choose Corporation if:

- You plan to **issue stock** or bring on investors who expect equity shares
- You may seek **venture capital or go public** in the future
- You want a **formal governance structure** with directors, officers, and shareholder meetings
- You prefer to keep profits in the company at the **corporate tax rate** rather than passing through to personal income

### Price difference:

Corporations cost $200 to $300 more than LLCs across all packages. This reflects the additional document preparation (bylaws, stock certificates, corporate minutes) and the more complex state filing requirements.

> **Cross-sell note:** Not sure which entity type fits your tax situation? Consult with a CPA or tax attorney before deciding. The entity type affects how you file taxes, pay yourself, and handle business profits. We form the entity — your tax advisor helps you choose the right one.

<details>
<summary>Frequently Asked Questions: LLC vs. Corporation</summary>

**Q: Can I convert an LLC to a Corporation later (or vice versa)?**
Yes, most states allow entity conversion. However, it involves new filings, potential tax consequences, and updated governance documents. It is simpler and cheaper to choose the right entity type upfront.

**Q: Which is better for a single-person business?**
For most solo entrepreneurs, an LLC is the better choice. It is simpler to manage, has fewer formalities, and offers the same liability protection. A Corporation makes sense if you specifically need to issue stock or want S-Corp tax treatment (which an LLC can also elect).

**Q: What about S-Corp election?**
Both LLCs and Corporations can elect S-Corp tax treatment with the IRS (Form 2553). This election affects how you pay yourself and can reduce self-employment taxes. It is a tax decision, not a formation decision — you can make it after your entity is formed.

</details>
