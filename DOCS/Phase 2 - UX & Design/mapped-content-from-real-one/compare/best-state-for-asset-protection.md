# Best State for Asset Protection

Asset protection through entity structuring is about creating legal barriers between your personal assets and potential creditors, litigants, and judgments. The state where you form your LLC or Corporation determines what protections are available, how strong they are, and how they have been tested in court. Four states stand out for asset protection: Wyoming, Nevada, South Dakota, and Delaware. Each offers different levels of protection, and the differences are significant enough to affect real outcomes in litigation.

## Wyoming: The Strongest Overall Protection

Wyoming provides what is broadly considered the most comprehensive asset protection framework for LLCs in the United States. The cornerstone is Wyoming Statute 17-29-503, which establishes the charging order as the exclusive remedy available to a creditor of an LLC member. This means a judgment creditor cannot:

- Force a sale of your LLC membership interest
- Seize your membership interest directly
- Take over management of the LLC
- Force distributions from the LLC

The creditor's only option is a charging order — essentially a lien on future distributions. If the LLC does not make distributions, the creditor receives nothing, while potentially owing taxes on income allocated to the charged interest (the so-called "phantom income" problem for creditors).

Critically, Wyoming extends this protection to single-member LLCs. This is a significant distinction. In states like Delaware and some interpretations of Nevada law, a single-member LLC may not receive charging order protection because a court may reason that there are no other members whose interests would be harmed by a more aggressive remedy. Wyoming's statute makes no distinction — all LLCs receive the same protection regardless of the number of members.

Wyoming also has a short fraudulent transfer lookback period of just one year under its Uniform Voidable Transactions Act, meaning assets transferred to a Wyoming LLC more than one year before a claim arises are generally beyond challenge. This is the shortest lookback period among the leading asset protection states.

## Nevada: Strong Protections with Higher Costs

Nevada offers robust asset protection, particularly through its strong director and officer liability shields — among the strongest in the country. Nevada also provides charging order protection for LLCs, though the application to single-member LLCs is less explicitly settled in case law compared to Wyoming.

Nevada's Revised Statutes provide that a charging order is the exclusive remedy for a judgment creditor of a multi-member LLC. For single-member LLCs, the protection exists but has not been as thoroughly tested in court. Most practitioners believe the protection applies, but the statutory language is not as definitive as Wyoming's.

Nevada's fraudulent transfer lookback period is two years — reasonable, but twice as long as Wyoming's one-year period. Nevada also imposes higher ongoing costs: the annual list of officers ($150), state business license (LLC $200/yr, Corp $500/yr), and higher formation fees overall.

For business owners who are physically located in Nevada or have specific reasons to be incorporated there, Nevada's asset protection is strong and well-established. For those choosing purely on the merits of protection, Wyoming offers equivalent or stronger protection at lower cost.

## South Dakota: Underrated but Limited Services

South Dakota has emerged as a strong asset protection jurisdiction, particularly for domestic asset protection trusts. The state provides charging order protection as the exclusive remedy for all LLCs (including single-member), has no state income tax, and imposes low annual fees.

However, South Dakota's corporate and LLC case law is less developed than Wyoming's or Nevada's, which means there are fewer court decisions to rely on when predicting how protections will hold up in contested litigation. The state is also less well-known among formation service providers, which can make it harder to find experienced registered agents and compliance services.

## Delaware: The Corporate Default with Asset Protection Gaps

Delaware is the default formation state for large corporations and venture-backed companies, but its asset protection framework has a significant gap for small business owners: single-member LLCs do not receive charging order protection. In the landmark case _Olmstead v. FTC_ — decided under Florida law but frequently cited in Delaware analysis — courts have allowed creditors to directly seize single-member LLC interests rather than being limited to charging orders.

Delaware's LLC Act does not clearly establish the charging order as the exclusive remedy for single-member LLCs, and practitioners generally advise that a single-member Delaware LLC provides no meaningful charging order protection. For the typical small business owner or entrepreneur operating as a sole proprietor through an LLC, this is a critical weakness.

Delaware also imposes a $300 annual franchise tax on LLCs and has a four-year fraudulent transfer lookback period — the longest among the states compared here.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is a charging order?**
A charging order is a court order that entitles a judgment creditor to receive distributions from an LLC that would otherwise go to the debtor-member. It does not give the creditor ownership, voting rights, or management control. In states where it is the exclusive remedy, it is the only tool available to creditors — they cannot seize the LLC interest directly.

**What is "phantom income" and why does it matter?**
When a charging order is in place, the creditor may be allocated taxable income from the LLC even if no cash is actually distributed. This creates a negative incentive for creditors to pursue charging orders against LLCs that do not make regular distributions, because the creditor may owe taxes on income they never received.

**Does asset protection work if I'm sued in my home state?**
The asset protection of your LLC is generally determined by the law of the state where the LLC is formed, not where the lawsuit is filed. However, courts can apply their own state's public policy in some circumstances. Forming in Wyoming provides the strongest starting position.

**Can I transfer existing assets into a Wyoming LLC?**
Yes, but timing matters. Transfers made after a claim has arisen (or when a claim is reasonably foreseeable) may be challenged as fraudulent transfers. Wyoming's one-year lookback period means that transfers completed more than one year before a claim are generally beyond challenge. Consult an attorney for specific guidance on your situation.

**Do I need both privacy and asset protection?**
They work together. Privacy prevents potential litigants from discovering what assets you own and where they are held. Asset protection provides legal barriers if they do discover and pursue your assets. The Gold package combines both.

**Is a Corporation or LLC better for asset protection?**
For most small business owners, an LLC provides superior asset protection because of the charging order framework. Corporations do not have an equivalent mechanism — shares of stock can be seized by creditors more readily than LLC membership interests in states like Wyoming.

**What about domestic asset protection trusts?**
Wyoming allows domestic asset protection trusts (DAPTs), which provide an additional layer of protection beyond entity structuring. A DAPT can hold your LLC membership interests, creating a trust-plus-LLC structure. This is an advanced strategy that should be implemented with the guidance of an asset protection attorney.

**Can I use a Series LLC for asset protection?**
Both Wyoming and Nevada allow Series LLCs, which let you create separate "series" within a single LLC, each with its own assets, members, and liabilities. This can be useful for real estate investors holding multiple properties. Each series is legally shielded from the liabilities of the others.

**Does forming in Wyoming protect me from lawsuits in my home state?**
The entity's asset protection framework is generally governed by the state of formation. However, if you conduct business in your home state, you may be subject to your home state's courts. Forming in Wyoming gives you the strongest foundation, but you should also maintain proper corporate formalities and carry adequate insurance.

**How does asset protection differ for Corporations vs LLCs?**
For most small business owners, LLCs provide superior asset protection because of the charging order framework. Corporate stock can be seized by a creditor more readily than LLC membership interests. The charging order mechanism is specific to LLCs and is one of the primary reasons LLCs are preferred for asset protection planning.
