# IRS Compliance for Offshore Structures

If you are a U.S. person with offshore entities, bank accounts, or trusts, you have IRS reporting obligations. Full compliance is not optional, and the penalties for non-reporting can exceed the value of the assets you are trying to protect. This guide explains every form you may need to file, organized by the type of offshore structure you hold.

## The Core Principle

Disclosing your offshore assets and structures to the IRS does not give the IRS the ability to seize those assets. Once assets are held outside the United States, the IRS becomes a creditor like any other. It must pursue collection through complex legal procedures in the foreign jurisdiction, facing the same barriers and protections that shield you from private creditors.

The goal is straightforward: comply fully with reporting requirements to avoid penalties, while your assets remain protected by the legal frameworks of the jurisdictions where they are held.

## Offshore Trust Compliance

Our [Bahamas Asset Protection Trust](/offshore/trust/) is designed to be tax-neutral under the Grantor Trust rules (U.S. Code 671). The settlor is treated as the tax owner of property transferred to the trust. As a result:

- The trust is disregarded for U.S. income tax purposes
- Income from the trust fund passes through to the settlor's Form 1040
- Transfers to the trust and distributions from the trust have zero tax consequences

### Required Forms for Trusts

**Form 3520-A** — Annual Information Return of Foreign Trust with a U.S. Owner
The trustee prepares this form, including applicable K-1 forms showing income attributed to or distributions made to U.S. persons. This is handled by the trust administration, not by you directly.

**Form 3520** — Annual Return to Report Transactions with Foreign Trusts
You (or your CPA) file this form to report any transfers to or distributions from the trust during the year. It is filed for the year you establish the trust and annually thereafter. This is an informational return only — no tax is paid with the filing.

### After the Settlor's Death

After the death of a U.S. settlor, the trust becomes a true foreign trust for tax purposes. The trust fund may grow tax-free for generations with no U.S. tax due and no U.S. reporting requirements, except to report any distributions made to U.S. beneficiaries. This is one of the most powerful long-term benefits of an offshore trust structure.

## Offshore Company Compliance: Pass-Through Election

For Nevis, BVI, and Hong Kong entities, we recommend electing pass-through tax treatment. This process includes:

- Filing Form SS-4 to obtain an EIN for the company
- Filing Form 8832 to elect Foreign Disregarded Entity (single-member) or Foreign Partnership (multi-member) status

Once the entity has pass-through status, the complex Controlled Foreign Corporation filing (Form 5471) is eliminated entirely. Compliance becomes comparable in complexity to a domestic LLC or partnership.

### Required Forms for Pass-Through Entities

**Form 8858** — Information Return of U.S. Persons with Respect to Foreign Disregarded Entities
Filed with your Form 1040 if your entity has single-member FDE status. Reports the activities and financial information of the foreign entity.

**Form 8865** — Return of U.S. Persons with Respect to Certain Foreign Partnerships
Filed with Form 1065 for entities with Foreign Partnership status. We recommend proactively filing a standard U.S. partnership return alongside Form 8865 to fulfill the offshore filing requirement cleanly.

## Offshore Company Compliance: CFC Filing

If your international corporation does not elect pass-through taxation, or is not eligible (such as a Panama Corporation), then U.S. shareholders face Controlled Foreign Corporation (CFC) reporting requirements.

### Required Forms for CFC Entities

**Form 5471** — Information Return of U.S. Persons with Respect to Certain Foreign Corporations
Filed annually with your Form 1040. This is the most complex offshore reporting form and reports the financial activities of the foreign corporation in detail.

**Form 926** — Return by a U.S. Transferor of Property to a Foreign Corporation
Filed when you initially transfer property (cash or other assets) to capitalize the foreign corporation. This is typically a one-time filing at formation, plus any subsequent capital contributions.

## Forms Required for All Offshore Structures

Regardless of whether you hold a trust, company, or both, U.S. persons with foreign financial assets and accounts are required to file:

**Form 8938** — Statement of Specified Foreign Financial Assets
Filed with your Form 1040. Reports foreign financial accounts, stock in foreign companies, interests in foreign trusts, and other specified foreign financial assets. Filing thresholds vary based on your filing status and whether you live in the U.S. or abroad.

**FBAR / FinCEN Form 114** — Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts
Filed electronically with FinCEN (not the IRS) by April 15, with an automatic extension to October 15. Required if the aggregate value of all foreign financial accounts exceeds $10,000 at any time during the calendar year.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

The IRS has made offshore reporting penalties a significant revenue source. Penalties are assessed per form, per year, and can include:

- **Form 3520/3520-A:** The greater of $10,000 or 35% of the gross value of trust distributions or transfers
- **Form 5471:** $10,000 per year, with additional penalties up to $50,000 for continued non-filing
- **Form 8865:** $10,000 per year
- **Form 8938:** $10,000 per year, up to $50,000 for continued non-filing
- **FBAR:** Up to $12,500 per year for non-willful violations; up to $100,000 or 50% of account balance for willful violations

These penalties apply per form, per entity, per year. A client with a trust and two offshore companies who fails to file for three years could face cumulative penalties exceeding $100,000. Compliance is far less expensive than the consequences of non-compliance.

## FAQ

**Do I need a special CPA for offshore reporting?**
Not necessarily, but your CPA should be familiar with the specific forms. Many general practitioners are increasingly comfortable with offshore reporting. If your CPA lacks experience in this area, we can provide referrals to specialists.

**What if I have not been filing these forms?**
The IRS has voluntary disclosure programs that allow taxpayers to come into compliance without facing criminal prosecution. We strongly recommend consulting with a tax attorney if you have unfiled forms. Coming forward proactively is always better than being discovered.

**Can the IRS see my offshore bank account balances?**
Through FATCA and the Common Reporting Standard (CRS), many foreign banks report U.S. account holders' information to the IRS. Attempting to hide accounts is increasingly futile and the penalties for non-disclosure are severe.

**Does filing FBAR mean I owe taxes on my offshore accounts?**
No. FBAR is an informational filing only. You owe taxes only on income earned in the accounts, which is reported through your standard tax return and applicable foreign entity forms.

**If I disclose everything, can the IRS just take my offshore assets?**
No. Disclosure does not equal seizure. The IRS becomes a creditor and must pursue collection through the foreign jurisdiction's legal system, the same system that protects you from any other creditor.

**When are these forms due?**
Most forms are due with your personal tax return (April 15, with extensions). FBAR is due April 15 with an automatic extension to October 15. Form 3520-A is due March 15. We send reminders for all applicable deadlines.

**What is the Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures program?**
The IRS offers this program for taxpayers who were non-willfully non-compliant with their offshore reporting obligations. It allows you to file the last three years of amended tax returns and six years of FBARs with reduced penalties. This is significantly better than waiting for the IRS to discover your non-filing, which can trigger willful penalties.

**Can my domestic CPA handle offshore reporting?**
Many domestic CPAs can handle the standard forms (8938, FBAR). For more complex structures involving Forms 5471, 8865, or 3520, you may benefit from a CPA with specific international tax experience. We can provide referrals.

**Do I need to file FBAR if my offshore account balance is very small?**
Yes, if the aggregate value of all your foreign financial accounts exceeds $10,000 at any point during the year. This is the combined total across all accounts — not per account. Even temporarily exceeding $10,000 triggers the requirement.

**What happens if I inherit an offshore trust or account?**
You inherit the reporting obligations along with the assets. You must file the applicable forms in the year you receive the inheritance or become a beneficiary. Failing to do so triggers the same penalties as any other non-filing.
