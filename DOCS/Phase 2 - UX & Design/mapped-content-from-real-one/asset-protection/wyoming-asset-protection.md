## What Is Wyoming Asset Protection?

Wyoming asset protection uses the state's LLC statutes to create a legal barrier between your personal assets and potential creditors. The strategy is straightforward:

You form a Wyoming LLC and transfer personal assets — cash, investment accounts, real estate, intellectual property — into the entity. You receive a membership interest in the LLC in exchange. The LLC now owns the assets. You maintain full control through the operating agreement, but legal ownership belongs to the entity.

If a creditor later obtains a judgment against you personally, they cannot reach the assets inside the LLC. Under Wyoming Statute 17-29-503, their only remedy is a charging order — a lien on distributions the LLC makes to you as a member. The creditor cannot force distributions, cannot seize LLC assets, cannot vote on management decisions, and cannot foreclose on your membership interest. The statute explicitly prohibits these remedies:

> _"Other remedies, including foreclosure on the judgment debtor's limited liability interest and a court order for directions, accounts and inquiries that the judgment debtor might have made are not available to the judgment creditor attempting to satisfy a judgment out of the judgment debtor's interest in the limited liability company and may not be ordered by the court."_ — Wyoming Statute 17-29-503(g)

This is not a loophole or an aggressive interpretation. It is the plain language of the statute, enacted by the Wyoming legislature and upheld by Wyoming courts.

## Why Wyoming Is the Strongest Jurisdiction

### Single-Member LLC Protection

This is Wyoming's defining advantage. In most states, courts have questioned whether charging order protection applies when an LLC has only one member. The reasoning is that a single-member LLC is essentially an alter ego of the individual, and restricting creditor remedies to a charging order serves no purpose when the debtor controls all distributions.

Wyoming eliminated this ambiguity. Statute 17-29-503(g) explicitly states that charging order protection applies to "any judgment debtor who may be the sole member." If you are the only owner of your LLC, Wyoming still protects your assets. This matters enormously, because most individual asset protection structures involve a single owner.

### No State Income Tax

Wyoming imposes no personal income tax, no corporate income tax, and no gross receipts tax. Investment income earned within your Wyoming LLC — dividends, interest, capital gains, rental income — is not subject to state-level taxation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does Wyoming really protect single-member LLCs?**
Yes. Wyoming Statute 17-29-503(g) explicitly states that charging order protection is the exclusive remedy even when the judgment debtor is "the sole member." This is the statute's plain language, not an interpretation. Wyoming is the only state that provides this level of explicit protection for single-member LLCs.

**What is a charging order?**
A charging order is a court order that gives a judgment creditor a lien on distributions made by the LLC to its members. The creditor does not gain ownership, voting rights, management authority, or access to LLC assets. They simply receive whatever distributions would have gone to the debtor-member — and the LLC is under no obligation to make distributions.

**Can I form a Wyoming LLC if I do not live in Wyoming?**
Absolutely. You do not need to live in Wyoming, visit Wyoming, or conduct business in Wyoming. The LLC is governed by Wyoming law regardless of where you reside. We serve clients in all 50 states.

**What is "phantom income" and how does it deter creditors?**
If a creditor holds a charging order against your LLC membership interest, they may be allocated taxable income from the LLC — even if no cash is actually distributed. The creditor would owe income tax on money they never received. This creates a strong financial incentive for the creditor to negotiate a settlement or release the charging order.

**Can a creditor pierce the veil of my Wyoming LLC?**
Veil piercing requires a creditor to prove that the LLC is a sham — that you did not maintain it as a separate entity, commingled personal and LLC funds, or used the LLC to perpetrate fraud. When your LLC is properly structured with a custom operating agreement, separate bank accounts, and proper recordkeeping, veil piercing is extremely difficult. Our formation process is designed to prevent these vulnerabilities.

**What happens if I need to access my assets?**
You maintain full control of your LLC and can make distributions to yourself at any time (assuming no charging order is in place). The operating agreement governs how distributions work. Your day-to-day access to funds held in LLC bank and investment accounts is unchanged — you simply access them through the LLC rather than personally.

**Is it too late to set up asset protection if I think I might be sued?**
Asset protection is most effective when established before any claim arises. Transferring assets after you are aware of a potential lawsuit can be challenged as a fraudulent transfer. However, every situation is different. Call us to discuss your specific circumstances — we can advise on what options remain available to you.
