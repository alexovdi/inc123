# LLC vs Living Trust

One of the most common questions we hear from clients is whether they need an LLC, a living trust, or both. It is a fair question — both are legal entities that hold assets, both are used in financial planning, and both are frequently recommended by attorneys and financial advisors. But they serve fundamentally different purposes, and confusing the two can leave critical gaps in your protection.

A living trust is an estate planning tool. It helps your family avoid probate and ensures a smooth transfer of assets after your death. An LLC is an asset protection tool. It shields your assets from creditors and lawsuits during your lifetime. A living trust provides zero protection from creditors. An LLC provides zero estate planning benefits.

For many of our clients at Incorporate123, the answer is both — and structured correctly, an LLC owned by a living trust provides the strongest combination of lifetime asset protection and seamless estate transfer. Here is how each works and when you need one or both.

## What Is a Living Trust?

A revocable living trust is a legal arrangement where you (the grantor) transfer assets to a trust that you also control (as trustee) during your lifetime. You can use, manage, and modify the assets freely while you are alive. When you die, the successor trustee you have named distributes the assets to your beneficiaries according to the trust's instructions — without going through probate.

Probate is the court-supervised process of distributing a deceased person's assets. It is public, often slow (6-18 months in many states), and expensive (fees typically range from 2-5% of the estate's value). A living trust avoids this entirely.

**What a living trust does:**
- Avoids probate for assets held in the trust
- Keeps your estate distribution private (unlike a will, which becomes public record)
- Provides for management of your assets if you become incapacitated
- Allows you to specify exactly how and when beneficiaries receive assets

**What a living trust does NOT do:**
- Protect assets from lawsuits or creditors during your lifetime
- Shield assets from a judgment entered against you
- Provide any tax benefits (for revocable trusts)
- Create a legal barrier between you and your assets

Because a revocable living trust can be modified or revoked at any time by the grantor, courts treat the trust's assets as belonging to the grantor. A creditor who obtains a judgment against you can reach assets in your revocable living trust just as easily as assets in your personal bank account. The trust is transparent to creditors — it provides no shield whatsoever.

## What Is an LLC?

A limited liability company is a business entity that holds and manages assets. When you transfer assets into an LLC, legal ownership shifts to the entity. You maintain control through your membership interest and the LLC's operating agreement, but you no longer own the assets personally.

The asset protection power of an LLC comes from [charging order protection](/asset-protection/charging-order-protection/). In Wyoming (Statute 17-29-503) and Nevada (NRS 86.401), a charging order is the exclusive remedy available to a judgment creditor who wants to collect from your LLC membership interest. The creditor cannot seize LLC assets, force distributions, vote on management decisions, or foreclose on your interest.

**What an LLC does:**
- Protects assets from personal creditors through charging order statutes
- Creates a legal separation between you and your assets
- Provides liability protection — claims against the LLC do not reach your personal assets
- Offers privacy when combined with nominee services (your name does not appear in public filings)

**What an LLC does NOT do:**
- Help your family avoid probate
- Provide for asset distribution after your death
- Name beneficiaries or successor managers automatically
- Address incapacity planning

## The Key Differences

| Feature | Living Trust | LLC |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary purpose** | Estate planning | Asset protection |
| **Creditor protection** | None (revocable trusts) | Strong (charging order) |
| **Probate avoidance** | Yes | No |
| **Privacy from creditors** | No | Yes (with nominee services) |
| **Estate distribution** | Yes — specifies beneficiaries | No — requires separate planning |
| **Incapacity planning** | Yes — successor trustee takes over | Limited — depends on operating agreement |
| **Lifetime control** | Full | Full |
| **Tax treatment** | Transparent (same as personal) | Varies (disregarded entity, partnership, or S-corp) |

The critical takeaway: these tools solve different problems. Using a living trust for asset protection leaves you exposed. Using an LLC for estate planning leaves your family facing probate.

## The Combined Approach: LLC Owned by the Trust

For clients who need both asset protection and estate planning — which is most clients with $500,000 or more in assets — the optimal structure uses both tools together.

Here is how it works:

1. **You establish a revocable living trust** with the help of an estate planning attorney. The trust names you as trustee and your spouse or another trusted person as successor trustee.
2. **You form a Wyoming or Nevada LLC** through Incorporate123. The LLC is structured with charging order protection, nominee services, and a custom operating agreement.
3. **The living trust owns the LLC membership interest.** Instead of you personally holding the membership interest, the trust holds it. You control the trust (and therefore the LLC) during your lifetime.
4. **Assets are held inside the LLC.** Cash, investments, real estate, and other assets are transferred into the LLC, where they are protected by charging order statutes.

**During your lifetime:** The assets inside the LLC are protected from personal creditors by charging order statutes. You maintain full control through the trust (which controls the LLC).

**After your death:** The LLC membership interest passes to your beneficiaries through the trust — no probate required. The successor trustee manages the transition according to your trust's terms. The LLC can continue holding and protecting the assets for your beneficiaries, or the assets can be distributed as specified.

This structure gives you the best of both tools: lifetime creditor protection from the LLC and seamless estate transfer from the trust.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does a living trust protect my assets from lawsuits?**
No. A revocable living trust provides no creditor protection. Because you can modify or revoke the trust at any time, courts treat the trust's assets as yours. A creditor can reach assets in your revocable living trust just as easily as assets held personally.

**Can an LLC avoid probate?**
Not by itself. An LLC membership interest is an asset that passes through your estate like any other. If you hold the membership interest personally, it will go through probate. If the membership interest is held by a living trust, it passes to your beneficiaries outside of probate.

**Why not just use an irrevocable trust for asset protection?**
An irrevocable trust can provide creditor protection, but you permanently give up control of the assets. You cannot modify the trust, change beneficiaries, or access the assets without trustee approval. Most people are not willing to surrender that level of control over their wealth. An LLC lets you maintain full control while providing strong creditor protection.

**Can my living trust own multiple LLCs?**
Yes. Many clients have their living trust own a holding LLC, which in turn owns multiple property LLCs or operating entities. This creates a clean structure that combines asset protection, liability isolation, and estate transfer in a single hierarchy.

**What happens to the LLC when I die?**
The LLC membership interest passes according to the terms of your living trust (if the trust owns the interest) or your will (if you hold the interest personally). The LLC itself continues to exist and hold assets. Your successor trustee or beneficiaries step into your role as members.

**Do I need an attorney to set up a living trust?**
Yes. We strongly recommend working with a qualified estate planning attorney in your state to establish your living trust. Trust law varies significantly by state, and the trust must be drafted to work properly with your LLC structure. We handle the LLC side; your attorney handles the trust.

**What if I already have a living trust but no LLC?**
This is one of the most common situations we see. You have estate planning in place but no creditor protection. We can form your LLC and work with your estate planning attorney to assign the membership interest to your existing trust.

**Can a creditor force the trust to revoke itself to access the LLC?**
In some jurisdictions, courts have ordered grantors of revocable trusts to take actions to satisfy judgments, since the grantor retains control. However, the creditor still faces charging order protection at the LLC level. Even if a court could somehow compel action at the trust level, the assets inside the LLC remain protected by Wyoming or Nevada charging order statutes.

**Is this structure legal?**
Absolutely. LLCs and living trusts are standard legal tools recognized in every state. Using them together is a well-established planning strategy recommended by estate planning and asset protection attorneys nationwide. The key requirement is that the structure must be established before any claims arise — not in response to existing or anticipated litigation.
