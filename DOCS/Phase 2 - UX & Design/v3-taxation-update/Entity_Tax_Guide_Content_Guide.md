### Section 1: How Business Entities Are Taxed

Overview of default tax treatment for each entity type. This is the foundational section — visitors who understand nothing about business taxation start here.

| Entity Type | Default Tax Treatment | IRS Form | Key Concept |
|-------------|----------------------|----------|-------------|
| Single-Member LLC | Disregarded entity — reported on Schedule C of personal return | Schedule C (Form 1040) | Simplest structure; all income is personal income |
| Multi-Member LLC | Partnership — each member receives K-1 | Form 1065 / Schedule K-1 | Income "passes through" to members proportionally |
| S-Corporation | Pass-through — shareholders receive K-1 | Form 1120-S / Schedule K-1 | Requires reasonable salary; distributions avoid SE tax |
| C-Corporation | Separate taxable entity — corporate tax rate | Form 1120 | Double taxation: corporate tax + dividend tax on distributions |

**Key concept to explain:** Pass-through taxation vs. double taxation. Pass-through entities (LLCs, S-Corps) are taxed once — income flows to the owner's personal return. C-Corps are taxed twice — once at the corporate level (21% federal) and again when profits are distributed as dividends. Most small businesses prefer pass-through for this reason.

**Content direction:** Use a simple visual or comparison table. Avoid IRS jargon where possible. Lead with "what this means for your wallet" rather than "what the IRS calls it."

### Section 2: The S-Corp Election

When it makes sense, how it works, and why it's a federal tax classification — not a state formation type.

| Element | Content Direction |
|---------|-------------------|
| What it is | An IRS tax election (Form 2553) that changes how an LLC or Corp is taxed — not a new entity type |
| When it makes sense | Generally when net self-employment income consistently exceeds $80K/year |
| How it works | Owner pays themselves a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll taxes) + takes remaining profit as distributions (not subject to SE tax) |
| Filing requirement | Form 2553 filed with IRS, due by March 15 for existing entities or within 75 days of formation |
| Key point | S-Corp election is federal tax only — it does not change your state formation, your privacy protections, or your LLC's legal structure |
| Limitations | Max 100 shareholders, single class of stock, no non-resident alien shareholders |

**Content direction:** The $80K threshold is approximate and widely cited by CPAs. Below that, the added accounting costs ($1,500–$3,000/year for payroll + S-Corp return) typically outweigh SE tax savings. Be specific about these numbers — visitors need concrete guidance, not "it depends."

### Section 3: Self-Employment Tax and Entity Choice

The 15.3% SE tax is often the single biggest tax surprise for new business owners. This section explains it clearly.

| Entity Type | SE Tax Treatment |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Single-Member LLC | 15.3% SE tax on all net income (12.4% Social Security up to $168,600 + 2.9% Medicare on all) |
| Multi-Member LLC | 15.3% SE tax on each member's share of net income |
| S-Corp (LLC or Corp with election) | SE tax only on reasonable salary; distributions are exempt from SE tax |
| C-Corporation | No SE tax on salary (employer pays half of FICA); dividends are not subject to SE tax but face double taxation |

**Content direction:** Use a concrete example. "If your LLC earns $120K net profit: as a standard LLC, you pay ~$18,360 in SE tax. With an S-Corp election and a $60K reasonable salary, you pay ~$9,180 in payroll taxes — saving ~$9,180. But you'll spend $1,500–$3,000 on the additional accounting, so net savings are $6,000–$7,600."

### Section 4: Wyoming and Nevada Tax Advantages

State-level tax implications of forming in WY or NV — and the critical caveat about operating in other states.

| State | Income Tax | Franchise Tax | Annual Fee | Key Advantage |
|-------|-----------|---------------|------------|---------------|
| Wyoming | None | None | $60/year (annual report) | Lowest total cost of any formation state |
| Nevada | None | None for LLCs; Commerce Tax for $4M+ revenue | $350/year (annual list) | No state income tax; strong corporate protections |
| California | Yes (up to 13.3%) | $800/year minimum for all LLCs | Varies | WY LLCs operating in CA still owe CA taxes + $800 franchise tax |
| Florida | None (personal) | 5.5% corporate income tax (Corps only) | $138.75/year (annual report) | No personal income tax; LLCs avoid corporate tax |

**Critical caveat (must be prominently displayed):** Forming in Wyoming does NOT eliminate your tax obligations in the state where you operate. If you live and work in California, your WY LLC's income is still subject to California state income tax plus the $800 minimum franchise tax. Wyoming formation provides privacy and asset protection advantages — not tax avoidance.

**Content direction:** This is where visitors most commonly hold misconceptions. Be direct: "Wyoming has no state income tax. But if you operate in California, you owe California taxes regardless of where your LLC is formed." Never imply that WY formation is a tax strategy.

### Section 5: Tax-Informed Entity Decision Framework

Profile-based recommendations that tie tax considerations back to formation decisions.

| Profile | Recommended Entity | Tax Rationale | Inc123 Package |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|----------------|
| Solo entrepreneur, <$80K net income | Single-Member LLC | Simplest structure; SE tax is manageable at this income level; S-Corp accounting costs outweigh savings | Wyoming Silver ($875) |
| Solo entrepreneur, >$80K net income | Single-Member LLC + S-Corp Election | SE tax savings justify added accounting costs; LLC structure maintained | Wyoming Silver ($875) + CPA referral |
| Privacy-focused entrepreneur | Single-Member LLC (Anonymous) | Same tax treatment as standard LLC; privacy is a formation decision, not a tax decision | Wyoming Gold ($1,275) |
| Multiple owners | Multi-Member LLC | Pass-through taxation; flexible profit allocation via operating agreement | Wyoming Silver or Gold |
| Planning to raise investment capital | C-Corporation | Required for most institutional investors; preferred stock structures available | Nevada Corporation |
| Real estate investor | LLC (per property) | Pass-through taxation; each property isolated; loss deductions available | Wyoming Gold ($1,275) |

**Content direction:** This section bridges tax education and formation decision. Each row should feel like "if you're this person, here's what makes sense." Always end with: "These are general guidelines. Consult a CPA for advice specific to your financial situation."
