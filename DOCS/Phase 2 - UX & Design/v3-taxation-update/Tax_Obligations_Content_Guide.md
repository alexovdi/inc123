## Page Purpose

Post-formation reference content helping business owners understand their ongoing tax filing requirements after entity formation. This page covers what you owe, when you owe it, and what forms to file — organized by entity type and state.

**Critical positioning:** This is compliance guidance, NOT tax preparation. The page clarifies what obligations exist so business owners can work with their CPA effectively. Incorporate123 handles formation, registered agent, and annual state filings — not tax returns.

**Relationship to Entity Tax Guide:** The Entity Tax Guide (`/formation/entity-tax-guide`) helps visitors choose an entity type before formation. This page covers what happens after formation — the ongoing tax obligations that come with each entity type. Together, they create a complete pre-and-post-formation tax education pathway.

### Section 1: Federal Filing Requirements by Entity Type

Overview of what the IRS requires from each entity type after formation.

| Entity Type | Annual Federal Return | Due Date | Key Filing Notes |
|-------------|----------------------|----------|-----------------|
| Single-Member LLC | Schedule C (with personal Form 1040) | April 15 | No separate business return; reported on personal return |
| Multi-Member LLC | Form 1065 (Partnership Return) + Schedule K-1 per member | March 15 | Each member reports their K-1 income on personal return |
| S-Corporation | Form 1120-S + Schedule K-1 per shareholder | March 15 | Must also file payroll taxes (Form 941 quarterly) for owner salary |
| C-Corporation | Form 1120 (Corporate Return) | April 15 | Separate from personal taxes; dividends reported on personal return |

**Content direction:** Present this as a clear reference table. Most visitors will scan for their entity type and want to know: what form, when it's due, and whether they need a CPA to file it. Single-member LLCs can often self-file; all other types typically require a CPA or tax professional.

### Section 2: State-Level Tax Obligations

State taxes are where most confusion occurs — especially for businesses formed in one state and operating in another.

| State | Income Tax | Franchise/LLC Tax | Annual State Filing | Key Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|--------------------|----|
| Wyoming | None | None | Annual Report ($60) | Simplest state. No income tax, no franchise tax. Only the annual report. |
| Nevada | None | Commerce Tax (only if revenue exceeds $4M) | Annual List of Officers ($350) | No income tax for most businesses. Higher annual filing cost than WY. |
| California | Up to 13.3% personal; 8.84% corporate | $800/year minimum franchise tax (all LLCs) | Franchise Tax Board filing + Secretary of State Statement of Information | $800 applies even if the LLC earns $0. Applies to WY/NV LLCs foreign-registered in CA. |
| Florida | None (personal); 5.5% corporate | None for LLCs | Annual Report ($138.75) | No personal income tax. Corps pay 5.5% on income over $50K. |

**Critical point (must be prominently displayed):** If your Wyoming LLC operates in California, you owe California taxes AND the $800 franchise tax — regardless of where the LLC is formed. Formation state determines privacy and asset protection benefits; operating state determines tax obligations.

**Content direction:** Use a callout box or warning-style element for the multi-state obligation point. This is the #1 misconception among formation clients.

### Section 3: Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

| Element | Content Direction |
|---------|-------------------|
| Who must pay | Anyone who expects to owe $1,000+ in federal taxes for the year |
| Due dates | Q1: April 15 · Q2: June 15 · Q3: September 15 · Q4: January 15 (following year) |
| How to calculate | Use Form 1040-ES. Pay 100% of prior year's tax liability or 90% of current year's expected liability to avoid penalties. |
| How to pay | IRS Direct Pay, EFTPS, or include with Form 1040-ES voucher |
| State estimates | States with income tax (e.g., California) have their own quarterly estimate requirements on similar schedules |

**Content direction:** Quarterly estimates catch many new business owners off guard. Emphasize that this applies to most LLC and S-Corp owners — not just high earners. A $50K net income LLC owner likely owes $1,000+ in federal taxes and should be making quarterly payments.

### FAQs (3)

| # | Question | Answer Direction |
|---|----------|-----------------|
| 1 | "Does my Wyoming LLC owe Wyoming business taxes?" | No. Wyoming has no state income tax, no LLC franchise tax, and no gross receipts tax. Your only Wyoming obligation is the $60 annual report fee. However, if you operate in another state, you owe that state's taxes. |
| 2 | "What is California's $800 franchise tax?" | California imposes an $800/year minimum franchise tax on every LLC formed or registered in the state, regardless of income. This applies to Wyoming or Nevada LLCs that foreign-register in California. It is due by the 15th day of the 4th month after formation, then annually. |
| 3 | "Do I need quarterly estimated tax payments?" | If you expect to owe $1,000 or more in federal taxes for the year, yes. Due dates are April 15, June 15, September 15, and January 15. Most LLC and S-Corp owners with net income above $10K–$15K will need to make quarterly payments to avoid underpayment penalties. |

## Writing Rules

1. Reference-style tone — clear, structured, scannable
2. Every section: disclaimer that this is educational, not tax advice
3. Use specific numbers ($800 CA franchise tax, $60 WY annual report, $1,000 quarterly threshold)
4. Organize by entity type first, then by state — visitors think "I have an LLC, what do I owe?" not "I'm in Wyoming, what's required?"
5. Always direct to CPA for tax return preparation and planning
6. Clearly delineate Inc123 scope vs. CPA scope — no ambiguity
