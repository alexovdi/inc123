# Best State for LLC Privacy

Choosing a state for business formation is one of the most consequential privacy decisions you will make as a business owner. The state you incorporate in determines what ownership information becomes public record, who can access it, and how difficult it is to trace an entity back to you personally. Four states are most commonly discussed in the context of privacy: Wyoming, Nevada, New Mexico, and Delaware. Each offers different levels of protection, at different costs, with different trade-offs.

## Wyoming: The Privacy Leader

Wyoming consistently ranks as the strongest state for LLC and corporate privacy. The state does not require an Initial List of Officers or Managers to be filed with the Secretary of State, which means there is no publicly searchable database of who runs or owns your entity. When combined with nominee services — where our names appear on the articles of organization rather than yours — Wyoming provides complete ownership privacy from formation through the life of the entity.

Beyond privacy, Wyoming offers structural advantages that reinforce asset protection. The state's LLC statute designates the charging order as the exclusive remedy available to a creditor seeking to reach a member's interest, which means a judgment creditor cannot force a sale of your LLC interest, seize your membership, or take over management. Wyoming also imposes no state income tax, no franchise tax, and charges only $60 per year for LLC annual reports — making it the most cost-effective privacy state for long-term entity maintenance.

The one limitation of Wyoming is that it does require an annual report, which means ongoing compliance is necessary to maintain good standing. This is a minor administrative task that we handle as part of our Gold package.

## Nevada: Strong but More Expensive

Nevada has long been marketed as the premier state for business privacy, and it does offer genuine protections — but its privacy structure requires more effort and costs more to maintain than Wyoming's. The critical difference is that Nevada requires an Initial List of Officers and Managers to be filed with the Secretary of State. This list is publicly searchable. Without nominee services, your name and address are readily accessible to anyone who searches the Nevada Secretary of State database.

With nominee services in place, Nevada can provide privacy comparable to Wyoming's. However, the cost is higher because of Nevada's additional filing requirements: the mandatory Initial List ($150/year), the State Business License (LLC $200/year, Corp $500/year), and higher registered agent fees. These second-tier costs add up to hundreds of dollars more per year compared to Wyoming.

Nevada does offer strong asset protection and director liability protections, and it remains a solid choice for business owners who have a specific reason to be incorporated in Nevada — such as physical operations in the state or existing business relationships. But for business owners choosing purely on the basis of privacy, Wyoming achieves the same result at lower cost.

## New Mexico: No Annual Report, but Weaker Protections

New Mexico is sometimes recommended for privacy because it does not require an annual report and does not collect ownership information on public filings. Formation is inexpensive, and the state imposes no ongoing compliance requirements. On paper, this looks appealing for privacy.

However, New Mexico has significant weaknesses. The state imposes a gross receipts tax, has a state income tax, and its LLC statute does not provide the charging order as an exclusive remedy. This means a creditor may have broader options for reaching your LLC interest in New Mexico than in Wyoming or Nevada. New Mexico also lacks the depth of favorable corporate case law that Wyoming and Nevada have built over decades. For business owners who need privacy as part of a broader asset protection strategy, New Mexico's cost savings are offset by weaker legal protections.

## Delaware: Prestige without Privacy Advantages

Delaware is the default choice for large corporations and venture-backed startups because of its well-developed Court of Chancery and extensive body of corporate case law. However, for small business owners and entrepreneurs, Delaware offers no meaningful privacy advantage over Wyoming while costing significantly more.

Delaware does not require disclosure of members or managers on LLC formation documents, which provides some baseline privacy. But the state charges a $300 annual franchise tax for LLCs (with no upper limit for corporations based on authorized shares), and its asset protection provisions are weaker than Wyoming's. Delaware's charging order protections apply only to multi-member LLCs — single-member LLCs receive no charging order protection at all, which is a critical gap for solo entrepreneurs.

For the typical Incorporate123 client — a small business owner or entrepreneur with assets to protect who values privacy — Delaware offers less protection at higher cost compared to Wyoming.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I form in a privacy state and still operate in my home state?**
Yes. You form in Wyoming (or Nevada), then file a foreign registration in the state where you conduct business. This is a standard, legal process used by millions of businesses.

**Does forming in Wyoming mean I avoid my home state's taxes?**
No. If you conduct business in your home state, you are subject to that state's tax obligations regardless of where your entity is formed. The privacy benefit is about ownership records, not tax avoidance.

**Is New Mexico really free to maintain?**
New Mexico does not require an annual report or franchise tax, but the state does impose a gross receipts tax on business conducted within New Mexico. The $0 maintenance cost only applies if the entity is not conducting business in New Mexico itself.

**Why don't you offer Delaware packages?**
Delaware's advantages are primarily relevant to large corporations, venture-funded startups, and businesses planning to go public. For our typical client — a small business owner or entrepreneur focused on privacy and asset protection — Wyoming provides stronger protections at lower cost.

**Can a creditor find out who owns my Wyoming LLC?**
Not through public records. Wyoming does not collect or disclose ownership information. With nominee services, the articles and annual reports show our nominees, not you. A creditor would need a court order directed at the LLC itself to compel disclosure of membership information.

**What if I already have a Nevada LLC?**
You can continue to maintain it. If you are paying for nominee services in Nevada, you already have privacy protection. The analysis above applies primarily to new formations where you are choosing a state for the first time.

**Do I need nominee services in every state?**
No. In Wyoming, nominee services provide maximum privacy but the state already has strong baseline privacy. In Nevada, nominee services are essential because the state requires public disclosure of officers. In New Mexico and Delaware, the states do not collect that information in the first place.

**What is the difference between privacy and anonymity?**
Privacy means keeping your ownership information off publicly searchable databases. Anonymity means being completely untraceable. Our services provide strong privacy — your name does not appear on any public filing. Complete anonymity against all forms of legal inquiry is not achievable because courts can compel disclosure through subpoenas. Our goal is to make your ownership information unavailable to casual searches, competitors, data brokers, and potential litigants who have not yet initiated legal proceedings.

**Can data brokers find my LLC ownership?**
Not through state records if you use nominee services. Data brokers aggregate publicly available information, and with nominees in place, there is no public record linking you to the entity. However, if you use the entity name in other public contexts (domain registrations, business directories, social media), that information may be collected separately.

**Do I need to disclose my LLC ownership to the IRS?**
Yes. The IRS requires beneficial ownership information on your tax return regardless of whether you use nominees. Nominee services protect privacy from public record searches, not from the IRS. Your tax professional will report the entity on your personal return as required.
