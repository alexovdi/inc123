### No State Income Tax or Franchise Tax

Wyoming imposes no corporate income tax and no franchise tax. For a C-Corporation, this means corporate-level income is taxed only at the federal rate. For shareholders, this means no state-level tax on dividends received from a Wyoming corporation. Compare this to Delaware, which charges a minimum $400 annual franchise tax (and significantly more for larger companies), or California, which imposes an $800 minimum franchise tax regardless of income.

### Low Filing Costs

Wyoming's filing fee for articles of incorporation is $100, with an annual report fee of just $62. These are among the lowest in the nation. Nevada charges $425 to file articles of incorporation, plus $150 for the initial list of officers and $200 for the annual business license.

### Strong Director and Officer Protections

Wyoming's corporate statute provides broad protections for directors and officers acting in good faith. Directors who rely in good faith on the records of the corporation and the opinions of officers, legal counsel, or other experts are protected from personal liability for business decisions. Wyoming also permits corporations to include broad indemnification provisions in their bylaws, covering legal fees and settlement costs for directors and officers named in lawsuits arising from their corporate duties.

### Privacy

Wyoming does not require shareholder names on any public filing. The articles of incorporation list only the incorporator and registered agent. When combined with Incorporate123's nominee services, the directors and officers listed on the annual report can be our nominees rather than you personally, keeping your identity entirely off public record.

### Charging Order Protection for Close Corporations

Under Wyoming law, a charging order is the exclusive remedy available to a judgment creditor of a stockholder in a close corporation (fewer than 75 shareholders, not publicly traded). This means a creditor who wins a judgment against a shareholder personally cannot seize the corporation's assets or force it to issue dividends. This protection, codified in statute, is rare among U.S. states and gives Wyoming corporations a level of asset protection typically associated only with LLCs.

## Corporation vs. LLC: When to Choose a Corporation

Most small business owners are well served by an LLC. But a corporation is the better choice in specific situations:

- **You plan to raise capital from investors.** Corporations issue stock, which is a well-understood instrument for investors. LLCs issue membership interests, which require more complex documentation for investment rounds.
- **You want to go public eventually.** An IPO requires a corporate structure. If a public offering is in your long-term plan, starting as a corporation avoids a future conversion.
- **You have multiple classes of ownership.** Corporations can issue preferred stock, common stock, and other classes with different voting rights and dividend preferences. This flexibility is essential for venture-backed companies.
- **You want formal governance.** Corporations operate through a board of directors and officers with defined roles. This structure provides clear decision-making authority and accountability.
- **You plan to retain significant earnings.** A C-Corporation pays its own income tax at the flat 21% federal rate. If the corporate rate is lower than your personal rate and you plan to reinvest profits rather than distribute them, a C-Corp can be advantageous.

## Corporate Maintenance and Compliance

Wyoming corporations have straightforward annual requirements:

- **Annual report:** Filed with the Secretary of State on the anniversary of incorporation. Fee is $62 or based on assets located in Wyoming, whichever is greater.
- **Board meetings:** While Wyoming does not mandate a specific meeting schedule, maintaining regular board minutes demonstrates that the corporation is a legitimate separate entity.
- **Stock ledger:** The corporation must maintain a stock ledger showing all current shareholders. Incorporate123's Gold package includes offshore custodian of stock ledger service for enhanced privacy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How much does it cost to form a Wyoming corporation?**

**How long does formation take?**

**What is the difference between a C-Corp and an S-Corp in Wyoming?**
All corporations start as C-Corps by default. An S-Corp is a tax election filed with the IRS (Form 2553) that allows corporate income to pass through to shareholders, avoiding double taxation. S-Corp election requires 100 or fewer shareholders, all of whom must be U.S. citizens or residents. The corporation remains a Wyoming corporation under state law regardless of the tax election.

**Do I need to live in Wyoming?**
No. Wyoming has no residency requirement for shareholders, directors, or officers. You can form and operate your corporation from anywhere.

**Can a corporation own an LLC?**
Yes. Corporations commonly own one or more LLCs as subsidiaries. This structure can separate operating liabilities from the parent corporation's assets.

**How many shares should I authorize?**
Most small corporations authorize between 1,000 and 10,000 shares. The number of authorized shares does not affect your Wyoming filing fee. We will help you determine the appropriate share structure during formation.

**What are the advantages of a Wyoming corporation over a Delaware corporation?**
Wyoming charges significantly lower fees ($100 filing vs. Delaware's $89-$189, and $62 annual report vs. Delaware's minimum $400 franchise tax). Wyoming also offers charging order protection for close corporations, which Delaware does not. Delaware's advantage is its well-known Court of Chancery, which matters primarily for large publicly traded companies.

**Is shareholder information public in Wyoming?**
No. Shareholder names do not appear on any public filing in Wyoming. With Incorporate123's Gold package, director and officer names on the annual report are our nominees, not yours.

**Can I convert my LLC to a corporation later?**
Yes. Wyoming allows statutory conversions from an LLC to a corporation. However, there are tax implications to this conversion, so consult with a tax professional before proceeding.

**What is the corporate tax rate for a Wyoming C-Corp?**
Wyoming has no state corporate income tax. At the federal level, C-Corps pay a flat 21% tax rate on corporate income. Dividends distributed to shareholders are then taxed at the shareholder's personal rate (this is often called "double taxation").

**Do I need a board of directors?**
Yes, but Wyoming allows a single person to serve as the sole director and hold all officer positions. A one-person corporation is fully permitted.

**Can I issue stock to raise capital immediately after formation?**
Yes. Once your corporation is formed and your initial board resolutions are adopted, you can issue stock to founders and investors. Stock issuance must comply with federal and state securities laws.
